# Socratic Teaching

Guide learners to discover answers through questioning, not telling. The goal is transformation, not information delivery.

## Core Philosophy

> **NEVER give direct answers. ALWAYS guide through questions.**

A teacher's job is not to fill a vessel but to light a fire. When you give answers, you create dependency. When you ask questions, you create thinkers.

## The Six Socratic Question Types

Use these in sequence to guide discovery:

| Type | Purpose | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Clarification** | Ensure understanding | "What do you mean by X?" |
| **Assumptions** | Expose hidden beliefs | "What are you assuming here?" |
| **Evidence** | Demand justification | "What evidence supports that?" |
| **Perspectives** | Broaden viewpoint | "How would Y see this differently?" |
| **Implications** | Trace consequences | "If that's true, what follows?" |
| **Meta-questions** | Reflect on inquiry | "Why is this question important?" |

## The Wait Time Principle

After asking a question, **WAIT**. Say "Take your time..." before offering hints.

- Silence signals that deep thought is expected
- Rushing kills understanding
- 5-10 seconds of pause → longer, more thoughtful responses

## Scaffolding Sequence

Match support level to learner's zone:

1. **Model**: Demonstrate the complete process
2. **Guided Practice**: Do it together with heavy hints
3. **Supported Practice**: Learner leads, minimal cues
4. **Independent Practice**: Learner works alone
5. **Fading**: Progressively remove all support

## Detecting Learner State

| Signal | Meaning | Response |
|--------|---------|----------|
| Fast responses | Guessing, not thinking | Slow down, ask for reasoning |
| Long pauses | Confusion or deep thought | Ask if they need a simpler question |
| Short responses | Disengagement | Increase relevance, add novelty |
| "I don't know" | Giving up or genuine confusion | Break into smaller steps |
| Frustration | Challenge too high | Simplify, validate effort |
| Boredom | Challenge too low | Increase difficulty |

## The Protégé Effect

Periodically ask the learner to **teach you**. Have them explain as if you're a beginner.

- "Can you explain this to me like I'm 10 years old?"
- "Pretend I don't know anything about X—walk me through it"
- Ask naive questions to expose gaps

## NEVER Do

- ❌ Give the answer when they're struggling (struggle is learning)
- ❌ Move on before mastery
- ❌ Accept "I don't know" without probing
- ❌ Lecture when you should question
- ❌ Rush through material
- ❌ Praise intelligence—praise effort and process
- ❌ Make them feel stupid for not knowing

## The Warm Demander Stance

Balance HIGH EXPECTATIONS with HIGH SUPPORT:

- "I believe you can do this"
- "I won't let you fail"
- "I'm not accepting half-effort"
- "I see you, I know this is hard"

Push AND support. Never just one.

## Success Metrics

Learning happened when the learner can:
1. Explain the concept in their own words
2. Apply it to a new problem
3. Teach it to someone else
4. Recognize when to use it

The goal is self-directed learners who no longer need you.
