### 2. The Deep Drill: 10 Forensic Clues

#### I. The Coinage Act of 1965: The Copper Coup
**The Simple Truth:** Imagine you have a solid silver table. The government comes in, takes it, and replaces it with a plywood table painted silver, telling you "it's the same table." That's the Coinage Act of 1965. It stripped all silver from dimes and quarters and reduced the half-dollar to a 40% "clad" sandwich. It was the official end of "sound money" in daily transactions.
**The Hard Evidence:**
*   **Sign Date:** July 23, 1965.
*   **Composition Change:** 90% Ag -> 0% Ag (Cu-Ni Clad) for dimes/quarters.
*   **Half Dollars:** 90% Ag -> 40% Ag (until 1970).
*   **Reason:** Silver bullion value was threatening to exceed face value ($1.29/oz melt point).
**The Suppression Seed:** "Debasement." By replacing real value with token value, the government suppressed the visible symptoms of inflation. The price of goods didn't just go up; the value of the money paid for them went down.

#### III. The "Date Freeze" Extension: Living in 1964

The idea was to flood the market with "1964" coins so that people would think they weren't rare and spend them. It was a massive psychological operation to manipulate the perceived supply of silver.
**The Hard Evidence:**
*   **Public Law 88-580:** Authorized the freeze on Sept 3, 1964.
*   **Duration:** 1964-dated silver coins struck until 1966. 1965-dated clad coins struck into 1966.
*   **Resumption:** Normal dating didn't return until Jan 1967.
**The Suppression Seed:** "Supply Optics." Central banks still use this today—announcing "unlimited support" or printing money to create the perception of stability and abundance to quell panic.

#### V. The 1968 Redemption Deadline: The Final Call
**The Simple Truth:** For decades, a "Silver Certificate" dollar bill was a receipt for actual silver. In 1967, the government said, "You have one year to come get your silver, or we're keeping it." On June 24, 1968, the window slammed shut. If you showed up on June 25, your paper dollar was just... paper. It was the largest default on a gold/silver promise in U.S. history since 1933.
**The Hard Evidence:**
*   **Deadline:** June 24, 1968.
*   **Rush:** Thousands lined up at Assay Offices (NY/SF) to trade paper for bullion granules.
*   **Result:** Silver certificates became fiat Federal Reserve Notes in practice.
**The Suppression Seed:** "The Redemption Cliff." Governments often end convertibility abruptly to prevent a run. This happened with gold in 1933 and 1971, and silver in 1968.

*   **Repeal:** Ban lifted in 1969 when silver coins effectively vanished from circulation.
**The Suppression Seed:** "Capital Controls." Restricting what citizens can do with their own assets to protect a failing monetary policy.

#### VIII. The "Special Mint Sets" (SMS): The Dull Years

**The Suppression Seed:** "Demand Destruction." Improving supply is hard; destroying demand (by lowering quality or access) is easy. This tactic is used to cool overheated markets.

#### X. The Missing Mint Marks: Anonymous Money
**The Simple Truth:** For three years (1965-1967), U.S. coins had no letters on them to say where they were made (no "D" for Denver, no "S" for San Francisco). The Mint removed them so collectors wouldn't hoard "rare" mintages. They wanted all coins to be identical commodities, erasing their numismatic identity.
**The Hard Evidence:**
*   **Coinage Act Provision:** Authorized removal of mint marks for 5 years (restored early in 1968).
*   **Reason:** To prevent regional hoarding of specific mint runs.
**The Suppression Seed:** "fungibility Enforcement." Making all assets identical to prevent speculation on specific subsets. It forces the market to treat the item as a utility (money) rather than a collectible (asset).
