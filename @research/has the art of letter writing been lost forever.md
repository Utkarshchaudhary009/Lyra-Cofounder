# "The Letter" — A 2.8-Minute Speech
*≈ 400 words | For a school audience — students and teachers*
*Speaker: 16-year-old student from Uttar Pradesh*
*Structure: "Your son, Pat" appears four times — each carrying more weight*

---

*[Walk to the mic. Pause. Then, quietly:]*

Your son, Pat.

*[Three seconds of silence.]*

Three words. Written by a man who would be dead by morning. In 1916, in a Dublin prison cell, a revolutionary named Pádraig Pearse asked for one piece of paper — and wrote to his mother.

*"My Dearest Mother… I am happy except for the great grief of parting from you."*

He was executed at dawn. His mother kept that letter for the rest of her life.

*[Pause.]*

But I haven't told you his last line. Not yet.

---

*[Shift. A touch of humor.]*

What does our school think a letter is? Chapter twelve. Subject line. Salutation. Yours faithfully. We write letters to the Municipal Commissioner about garbage on MG Road — letters we will never send, to people we will never meet, about problems we did not choose.

*[Let the room laugh.]*

Yours faithfully. Yours sincerely. That is what we are taught. No one teaches us *Your son, Pat.*

*[Pause.]*

A real letter was a person choosing — carefully, slowly — what they wished another human soul to know about the content inside of their heart. That is not communication. That is communion.

---

*[Slower.]*

Long before Pearse, someone understood this. Ram could not reach Sita. So he placed his ring in Hanuman's hand — no paper, no ink — just a ring and a promise: *I am coming for you.*

Sita held that ring. Not for the gold. For the proof that someone chose to reach across an ocean for her.

That ring was shree Ram's version of *Your son, Pat.* No formula. Just: *You are not forgotten.*

---

I went home. Opened every drawer, every almari, every old suitcase. Looking for one letter that ended like Pearse's.

*[Pause.]*

I found nothing.

*[Silence.]*

Four generations. Not one *Your son.* Not one *Your daughter.* Not one word that says: *I was thinking of you.*

---

*[Build.]*

We send three hundred messages a day and say nothing.
We reach anyone on earth in two seconds — and reach no one.
We keep every screenshot, every reel — and nothing that matters.

**Show me a text message that will make someone weep in a hundred years.**

*[Silence.]*

You cannot. But Sita could hold a ring. And Margaret Pearse could hold a letter.

---

*[Slow. Final.]*

Pearse's last line — the one I held back:

*"I will call to you in my heart at the last moment."*

And then — three words that outlived a revolution:

**Your son, Pat.**

*[Three seconds.]*

Tonight, I am going to write a letter. And sign it — not *Yours faithfully,* not *Yours sincerely* — just my name.

I am asking you to do the same.

Because the signature — is the letter.
And the Letter itself - is the message.

---

*[Hold eye contact. Walk away. Say nothing.]*

---

### Speaker's Notes

**Pádraig Pearse** (1879–1916) was an Irish revolutionary, poet, and educator who led the 1916 Easter Rising against British rule. Executed by firing squad at Kilmainham Gaol, Dublin, May 3, 1916. His last letter to his mother is archived at the **Kilmainham Gaol Museum** and the **National Library of Ireland**. Full letter available at letters1916.ie.

**The Ramayana reference** draws from the Sundara Kanda. Ram gave Hanuman his signet ring (*anguthiya*) to carry to Sita in Lanka as proof of identity and hope. Sita gave Hanuman her *chudamani* in return — her own letter in reply.

### Performance Notes

**"Your son, Pat"** appears four times. Each must sound different:
1. **Opening** — whispered, unexplained. The audience doesn't know what it means.
2. **After school critique** — weighted, contrasted against "Yours faithfully."
3. **After Ramayana** — reverent, connecting two civilizations.
4. **Closing** — full voice, full weight. The climax. Spoken like a verdict.

**Timing:** ~400 spoken words. At a measured pace with pauses, fits within 2 minutes 50 seconds. Rehearse with a stopwatch — if running long, slightly increase pace during the school critique section (it's the natural "faster" section). Never rush the Pearse quotes or the pauses.
