# "The Pothole" — A 2.8-Minute Speech
*≈ 400 words | Topic: The Role of Youth in Local Governance and Policy-Making*
*Speaker: 16-year-old student from Uttar Pradesh*
*Structure: Full Circle — a pothole opens and closes the speech, transformed in meaning*

---

*[Walk to the mic. Don't smile.]*

There is a pothole outside Gate 3 of this school. It has been there for three years. It is so old, it has a name. We call it *the lake.*

*[Let the room laugh. Then drop the humor.]*

Eleven months ago, I did what we are taught to do. I wrote a formal letter to the Municipal Corporation. Complaint number 47,832.

*[Pause.]*

The pothole is deeper now. My complaint has not moved.

*[Pause.]*

Nobody ignored me because my complaint was wrong. They ignored me because I am sixteen. And in this country, sixteen-year-olds do not *govern.* We are governed. We do not make policy. We are the subject of policy. We don't sit in the room where decisions happen. We sit in the textbook — Chapter 7, Local Self-Government, five marks, long answer.

*[Let that land.]*

---

*[Shift. Stand taller. Data now — but make it burn.]*

Sixty-five percent of India is under thirty-five. We are the youngest nation on earth. The average age of a Member of Parliament is fifty-five. The people deciding what your city looks like in 2040 will not be alive in 2040.

*[Pause.]*

We build policy for a world that no longer exists — and we wonder why nothing works.

---

*[Lean in.]*

I am not saying sixteen-year-olds should run the government. I am saying we should be in the room when decisions are made. Because we see things they do not see.

*[Anaphora — look at a different part of the audience for each line.]*

We see the broken streetlight we walk past at 7 AM.
We see the bus stop with no bench our grandmothers wait at.
We see the playground that became a parking lot while no one was looking.

*[Pause.]*

We are not too young to notice. Why are we too young to fix?

---

In Finland, every municipality has a youth council — by law. In Kerala, Bal Panchayats give children a formal voice in village governance. It is not a fantasy. It is already happening. Just not here.

Not yet.

---

*[Slow. Every word a footstep now.]*

That pothole outside Gate 3 is still there. Same crater. Same silence.

But I am not the same person who wrote that complaint.

I no longer want to write complaints. I want to write policy.

And if no one gives me a seat in that room — I will build my own chair, carry it in, and sit down.

*[Pause.]*

I am sixteen. And I am not asking for permission.

---

*[Hold eye contact. Three seconds. Walk away. Say nothing.]*

---

### Speaker's Notes

**Statistics used:**
- **65% of India under 35:** Based on Census of India and UN Population Division data. India's median age is approximately 28.4 years, making it one of the youngest nations globally.
- **Average MP age ~55:** Analyses of Lok Sabha composition consistently show the average age of elected MPs in the mid-50s range. Verifiable through PRS Legislative Research and Election Commission data.
- **"Will not be alive in 2040":** Rhetorical framing of the age gap, not a literal actuarial claim. The point: policy-makers are building a future they statistically will not inhabit.

**International examples:**
- **Finland:** The Finnish Youth Act (*Nuorisolaki*) requires every municipality to establish a youth council (*nuorisovaltuusto*) to ensure young people's participation in local decision-making.
- **Kerala Bal Panchayats:** Children's panchayats (*Bal Sabhas*) have been established in multiple Indian states with UNICEF support. Kerala's Kudumbashree and local self-government initiatives have incorporated formal child and youth participation mechanisms.

### Performance Notes

**Tone map:**
1. **Opening (pothole)** — Dry, deadpan humor. "The lake" must be thrown away casually, not performed. The room will find it funny because you DIDN'T try to be funny.
2. **Complaint section** — Controlled frustration. Not angry yet. Just tired.
3. **"I am sixteen" passage** — This is where the anger enters. Each sentence gets slightly louder, slightly harder. "We sit in the textbook" should land like an accusation.
4. **Statistics** — Delivered as facts, not opinions. Let the numbers do the emotional work. "Will not be alive in 2040" should be said flatly, almost clinically. The audience will feel the weight because you DIDN'T.
5. **"We see" anaphora** — Physical movement. Look LEFT for streetlight. Look CENTER for bus stop. Look RIGHT for playground. The audience should feel you pointing at THEIR world.
6. **Closing** — The temperature drops. Slower. Quieter. "I want to write policy" is the turn. "Build my own chair" is the image. "Not asking for permission" is the detonation.

**Timing:** ~365 spoken words. At a measured pace with pauses, fits within 2 minutes 50 seconds. The fastest section is the statistics paragraph — deliver it with momentum. The slowest section is the close — every pause there is earned.

### Playbook Techniques Used

| Technique | Where |
|---|---|
| Cold Open (Strategy 1) | "There is a pothole outside Gate 3..." |
| Controlled Humor (Technique 5) | "We call it *the lake*" |
| Shocking Statistic, Reframed (Strategy 3) | "Will not be alive in 2040" |
| Concrete > Abstract (Technique 4) | Streetlight, bus stop, playground — not "youth engagement" |
| Anaphora (Technique 7) | "We see... We see... We see..." |
| Rule of Three (Technique 3) | Three "we see" lines; complaints → policy → chair |

| Quiet Exit (Closing Strategy 6) | Walk away. No "thank you." |
