# Chapter 1: Mass Percent (w/w%)
## Part I: The Foundations

---

## 🎯 Stage 1: The Core Idea

### What is Mass Percent?

Imagine you make a glass of lemonade. You take 10 grams of sugar and dissolve it in 90 grams of water. Now someone asks: "How sweet is this lemonade?"

You could say "it has 10 grams of sugar." But that's useless — 10 grams in a glass vs 10 grams in a swimming pool are very different things. What matters is **how much sugar compared to the total**.

**Mass percent answers this question:**

> Out of every 100 grams of solution, how many grams are solute?

In your lemonade: total solution = 10 + 90 = 100 g. Sugar = 10 g. So mass percent = **10%**.

That's it. No moles. No volume. Just mass ratios.

### Why It Matters

- **Commercial products** list concentration in mass percent: "37% HCl", "98% H₂SO₄"
- **Temperature-independent**: Since we're using mass (not volume), heating or cooling the solution doesn't change the mass percent
- **Gateway concept**: Understanding this makes molality, ppm, and mole fraction much easier

## 🔬 Stage 2: The Formula Lab

### The Formula

```
                    Mass of solute
Mass % (w/w%) = ─────────────────── × 100
                   Mass of solution
```

### Understanding Every Piece

| Variable | What it means | Common trap |
|----------|---------------|-------------|
| Mass of solute | Mass of the substance being dissolved (in grams) | Students sometimes confuse solute and solvent |
| Mass of solution | Mass of solute + mass of solvent (total) | NOT mass of solvent alone! |
| × 100 | Converts fraction to percentage | Forgetting this gives you mass fraction, not mass percent |

### The Three Forms of the Same Formula

Depending on what's given, you'll rearrange:

**Form 1: Find mass percent**
```
w/w% = (W_solute / W_solution) × 100
```

**Form 2: Find mass of solute**
```
W_solute = (w/w% × W_solution) / 100
```

**Form 3: Find mass of solution**
```
W_solution = (W_solute × 100) / w/w%
```

### Key Relationship
```
W_solution = W_solute + W_solvent

Therefore: W_solvent = W_solution − W_solute
```

> **⚠️ Common Mistake:** Students write `Mass of solute / Mass of solvent` in the formula. The denominator is **solution** (total), not solvent. This single mistake costs marks in every exam.

### Type 1: Direct Calculation — Given masses, find w/w%

**The Pattern:** You're given mass of solute and mass of solution (or solvent). Find w/w%.

#### Solved Example 1.1
**Q:** 15 g of NaCl is dissolved in 85 g of water. Find the mass percent of NaCl in the solution. 🟢

**Solution:**
```
Step 1: Identify solute and solvent
    Solute = NaCl = 15 g
    Solvent = Water = 85 g

Step 2: Calculate mass of solution
    W_solution = W_solute + W_solvent = 15 + 85 = 100 g

Step 3: Apply formula
    w/w% = (W_solute / W_solution) × 100
    w/w% = (15 / 100) × 100
    w/w% = 15%

Answer: 15%
```

**Why this works:** The question gave us solute and solvent masses separately. We first found the total (solution mass), then applied the formula directly.

### Type 2: Reverse — Given w/w% and total mass, find mass of solute

**The Pattern:** You know the concentration and total amount. Find how much solute is present.

#### Solved Example 1.2
**Q:** A 500 g solution of glucose is 18% by mass. How much glucose is present in the solution? 🟢

**Solution:**
```
Step 1: Identify what's given
    w/w% = 18%
    W_solution = 500 g
    W_solute = ?

Step 2: Rearrange formula
    W_solute = (w/w% × W_solution) / 100

Step 3: Calculate
    W_solute = (18 × 500) / 100
    W_solute = 9000 / 100
    W_solute = 90 g

Answer: 90 g of glucose
```

### Type 3: Finding mass of solvent from w/w%

**The Pattern:** Given w/w% and one mass → find the other mass, specifically the solvent.

#### Solved Example 1.3
**Q:** A 20% (w/w) solution of NaCl has a total mass of 300 g. Find the mass of water (solvent). 🟢

**Solution:**
```
Step 1: Find mass of solute
    W_solute = (20 × 300) / 100 = 60 g

Step 2: Find mass of solvent
    W_solvent = W_solution − W_solute
    W_solvent = 300 − 60 = 240 g

Answer: 240 g of water
```

### Type 4: Making a solution — How much solute to add?

**The Pattern:** "I want to make X grams of Y% solution. How much solute do I need?"

#### Solved Example 1.4
**Q:** How much NaOH is needed to prepare 500 g of a 10% (w/w) NaOH solution? How much water is needed? 🟢

**Solution:**
```
Step 1: Find mass of NaOH needed
    W_solute = (w/w% × W_solution) / 100
    W_solute = (10 × 500) / 100 = 50 g

Step 2: Find mass of water needed
    W_solvent = 500 − 50 = 450 g

Answer: Dissolve 50 g NaOH in 450 g water
```

**Why this works:** This is Type 2 in disguise — you're just finding mass of solute from a desired concentration.

### Type 5: Dissolving a solid in a given amount of water

**The Pattern:** "I dissolved X g of solute in Y g of water." — Find w/w%.

**Key difference from Type 1:** Here you're given solute + solvent (not solution). You must add them first.

#### Solved Example 1.5
**Q:** 20 g of CuSO₄ is dissolved in 480 g of water. Calculate the mass percent. 🟢

**Solution:**
```
Step 1: Calculate mass of solution
    W_solution = 20 + 480 = 500 g

Step 2: Calculate w/w%
    w/w% = (20/500) × 100 = 4%

Answer: 4%
```

> **⚠️ Common Mistake:** Using 480 (solvent mass) instead of 500 (solution mass) in the denominator. Remember: the denominator is ALWAYS the total solution.

### Type 6: Density Connection — Finding mass per unit volume from w/w%

**The Pattern:** Given w/w% and density of solution → find how many grams of solute per litre (or per mL).

**This type bridges mass percent to molarity.** It is the most important bridge concept.

#### Solved Example 1.6
**Q:** An aqueous solution of H₂SO₄ is 49% by mass and has a density of 1.4 g/mL. Calculate the mass of H₂SO₄ present in 500 mL of this solution. 🟡

**Solution:**
```
Step 1: Find mass of 500 mL solution using density
    d = mass/volume
    mass of solution = d × V = 1.4 × 500 = 700 g

Step 2: Find mass of H₂SO₄ using w/w%
    W_solute = (49/100) × 700 = 343 g

Answer: 343 g of H₂SO₄
```

**Why this works:** Density converts volume → mass. Once we have mass of solution, w/w% gives us mass of solute directly.

### Type 7: Commercial Solution Analysis

**The Pattern:** Real commercial chemical labels give w/w% and density. You decode them.

#### Solved Example 1.7
**Q:** A commercial bottle of hydrochloric acid states: "37% HCl, density = 1.19 g/mL". 
(a) What does "37% HCl" mean?
(b) How many grams of HCl are in every 100 g of this solution?
(c) How many grams of water are in every 100 g of this solution? 🟢

**Solution:**
```
(a) "37% HCl" means: In every 100 g of this solution, 
    37 g is HCl and the rest is water.

(b) 37 g of HCl per 100 g of solution. (Direct from definition)

(c) Water = 100 − 37 = 63 g per 100 g of solution.
```

#### Solved Example 1.7B
**Q:** Concentrated sulphuric acid is 98% H₂SO₄ by mass (d = 1.84 g/mL). A student needs 49 g of pure H₂SO₄. What volume of concentrated acid should they measure out? 🟡 ⭐

**Solution:**
```
Step 1: Find mass of solution containing 49 g H₂SO₄
    w/w% = (W_solute / W_solution) × 100
    98 = (49 / W_solution) × 100
    W_solution = (49 × 100) / 98 = 50 g

Step 2: Convert mass → volume using density
    V = mass / density = 50 / 1.84 = 27.17 mL

Answer: Measure out 27.17 mL of concentrated H₂SO₄
```

### Type 8: Temperature Independence — Conceptual

**The Pattern:** Why doesn't mass percent change with temperature?

#### Solved Example 1.8
**Q:** Explain why mass percent is temperature-independent, while molarity is temperature-dependent. 🟢

**Solution:**
```
Mass percent uses ONLY mass (grams) in both numerator and denominator:
    w/w% = (mass of solute / mass of solution) × 100

Mass does NOT change when temperature changes — 
heating a solution doesn't create or destroy matter.

Molarity uses VOLUME in the denominator:
    M = moles of solute / volume of solution (in L)

Volume DOES change with temperature — 
liquids expand when heated and contract when cooled.

Therefore:
    - Mass % → Temperature INDEPENDENT ✅
    - Molarity → Temperature DEPENDENT ✅
```
