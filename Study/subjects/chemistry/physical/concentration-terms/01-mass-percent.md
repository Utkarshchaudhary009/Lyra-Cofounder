# Chapter 1: Mass Percent (w/w%)
## Part I: The Foundations

---

## 🎯 Stage 1: The Core Idea

### What is Mass Percent?

Imagine you make a glass of lemonade. You take 10 grams of sugar and dissolve it in 90 grams of water. Now someone asks: "How sweet is this lemonade?"

You could say "it has 10 grams of sugar." But that's useless — 10 grams in a glass vs 10 grams in a swimming pool are very different things. What matters is **how much sugar compared to the total**.

**Mass percent answers this question:**

> Out of every 100 grams of solution, how many grams are solute?

In your lemonade: total solution = 10 + 90 = 100 g. Sugar = 10 g. So mass percent = **10%**.

That's it. No moles. No volume. Just mass ratios.

### Why It Matters

- **Commercial products** list concentration in mass percent: "37% HCl", "98% H₂SO₄"
- **Temperature-independent**: Since we're using mass (not volume), heating or cooling the solution doesn't change the mass percent
- **Gateway concept**: Understanding this makes molality, ppm, and mole fraction much easier

---

## 🔬 Stage 2: The Formula Lab

### The Formula

```
                    Mass of solute
Mass % (w/w%) = ─────────────────── × 100
                   Mass of solution
```

### Understanding Every Piece

| Variable | What it means | Common trap |
|----------|---------------|-------------|
| Mass of solute | Mass of the substance being dissolved (in grams) | Students sometimes confuse solute and solvent |
| Mass of solution | Mass of solute + mass of solvent (total) | NOT mass of solvent alone! |
| × 100 | Converts fraction to percentage | Forgetting this gives you mass fraction, not mass percent |

### The Three Forms of the Same Formula

Depending on what's given, you'll rearrange:

**Form 1: Find mass percent**
```
w/w% = (W_solute / W_solution) × 100
```

**Form 2: Find mass of solute**
```
W_solute = (w/w% × W_solution) / 100
```

**Form 3: Find mass of solution**
```
W_solution = (W_solute × 100) / w/w%
```

### Key Relationship
```
W_solution = W_solute + W_solvent

Therefore: W_solvent = W_solution − W_solute
```

> **⚠️ Common Mistake:** Students write `Mass of solute / Mass of solvent` in the formula. The denominator is **solution** (total), not solvent. This single mistake costs marks in every exam.

### Type 1: Direct Calculation — Given masses, find w/w%

**The Pattern:** You're given mass of solute and mass of solution (or solvent). Find w/w%.

#### Solved Example 1.1
**Q:** 15 g of NaCl is dissolved in 85 g of water. Find the mass percent of NaCl in the solution. 🟢

**Solution:**
```
Step 1: Identify solute and solvent
    Solute = NaCl = 15 g
    Solvent = Water = 85 g

Step 2: Calculate mass of solution
    W_solution = W_solute + W_solvent = 15 + 85 = 100 g

Step 3: Apply formula
    w/w% = (W_solute / W_solution) × 100
    w/w% = (15 / 100) × 100
    w/w% = 15%

Answer: 15%
```

**Why this works:** The question gave us solute and solvent masses separately. We first found the total (solution mass), then applied the formula directly.

### Type 2: Reverse — Given w/w% and total mass, find mass of solute

**The Pattern:** You know the concentration and total amount. Find how much solute is present.

#### Solved Example 1.2
**Q:** A 500 g solution of glucose is 18% by mass. How much glucose is present in the solution? 🟢

**Solution:**
```
Step 1: Identify what's given
    w/w% = 18%
    W_solution = 500 g
    W_solute = ?

Step 2: Rearrange formula
    W_solute = (w/w% × W_solution) / 100

Step 3: Calculate
    W_solute = (18 × 500) / 100
    W_solute = 9000 / 100
    W_solute = 90 g

Answer: 90 g of glucose
```

### Type 3: Finding mass of solvent from w/w%

**The Pattern:** Given w/w% and one mass → find the other mass, specifically the solvent.

#### Solved Example 1.3
**Q:** A 20% (w/w) solution of NaCl has a total mass of 300 g. Find the mass of water (solvent). 🟢

**Solution:**
```
Step 1: Find mass of solute
    W_solute = (20 × 300) / 100 = 60 g

Step 2: Find mass of solvent
    W_solvent = W_solution − W_solute
    W_solvent = 300 − 60 = 240 g

Answer: 240 g of water
```


**Why this works:** This is Type 2 in disguise — you're just finding mass of solute from a desired concentration.

### Type 5: Dissolving a solid in a given amount of water

**The Pattern:** "I dissolved X g of solute in Y g of water." — Find w/w%.

**Key difference from Type 1:** Here you're given solute + solvent (not solution). You must add them first.

#### Solved Example 1.5
**Q:** 20 g of CuSO₄ is dissolved in 480 g of water. Calculate the mass percent. 🟢

**Solution:**
```
Step 1: Calculate mass of solution
    W_solution = 20 + 480 = 500 g

Step 2: Calculate w/w%
    w/w% = (20/500) × 100 = 4%

Answer: 4%
```

> **⚠️ Common Mistake:** Using 480 (solvent mass) instead of 500 (solution mass) in the denominator. Remember: the denominator is ALWAYS the total solution.

### Type 6: Density Connection — Finding mass per unit volume from w/w%

**The Pattern:** Given w/w% and density of solution → find how many grams of solute per litre (or per mL).

**This type bridges mass percent to molarity.** It is the most important bridge concept.

#### Solved Example 1.6
**Q:** An aqueous solution of H₂SO₄ is 49% by mass and has a density of 1.4 g/mL. Calculate the mass of H₂SO₄ present in 500 mL of this solution. 🟡

**Solution:**
```
Step 1: Find mass of 500 mL solution using density
    d = mass/volume
    mass of solution = d × V = 1.4 × 500 = 700 g

Step 2: Find mass of H₂SO₄ using w/w%
    W_solute = (49/100) × 700 = 343 g

Answer: 343 g of H₂SO₄
```

**Why this works:** Density converts volume → mass. Once we have mass of solution, w/w% gives us mass of solute directly.

### Type 7: Commercial Solution Analysis

**The Pattern:** Real commercial chemical labels give w/w% and density. You decode them.

#### Solved Example 1.7
**Q:** A commercial bottle of hydrochloric acid states: "37% HCl, density = 1.19 g/mL". 
(a) What does "37% HCl" mean?
(b) How many grams of HCl are in every 100 g of this solution?
(c) How many grams of water are in every 100 g of this solution? 🟢

**Solution:**
```
(a) "37% HCl" means: In every 100 g of this solution, 
    37 g is HCl and the rest is water.

(b) 37 g of HCl per 100 g of solution. (Direct from definition)

(c) Water = 100 − 37 = 63 g per 100 g of solution.
```

#### Solved Example 1.7B
**Q:** Concentrated sulphuric acid is 98% H₂SO₄ by mass (d = 1.84 g/mL). A student needs 49 g of pure H₂SO₄. What volume of concentrated acid should they measure out? 🟡 ⭐

**Solution:**
```
Step 1: Find mass of solution containing 49 g H₂SO₄
    w/w% = (W_solute / W_solution) × 100
    98 = (49 / W_solution) × 100
    W_solution = (49 × 100) / 98 = 50 g

Step 2: Convert mass → volume using density
    V = mass / density = 50 / 1.84 = 27.17 mL

Answer: Measure out 27.17 mL of concentrated H₂SO₄
```

| 1.7b | "40% formaldehyde solution (formalin), d = 1.09 g/mL". Find mass of HCHO in 500 mL. | 🟡 |
| 1.7c | Concentrated H₂SO₄ (98%, d = 1.84 g/mL). A student needs exactly 196 g of H₂SO₄. What volume of conc. acid is needed? ⭐ | 🟡 |
| 1.7d | A bottle of commercial bleach reads "5.25% NaOCl by mass, d = 1.08 g/mL". How many grams of sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl) are present in a 400 mL cup of this bleach? | 🟡 |
| 1.7e | Concentrated ammonia solution is sold as "28% NH₃, d = 0.90 g/mL". A cleaning formulation requires exactly 126 g of pure NH₃. What volume of the concentrated ammonia should be used? | 🟡 |
| 1.7f | An industrial solvent cleaner is labeled "60% IPA by mass, d = 0.85 g/mL". Calculate the mass of water present in exactly 3 litres of the cleaner, assuming water is the only other component. ⭐ | 🔴 |

<details>
<summary>💡 Detailed Solutions for Type 7</summary>

**1.7a:**
- **Given:** $68\%\text{ w/w}$, $d = 1.41\text{ g/mL}$.
- **Mass of 1 L (1000 mL) solution:** $1000 \times 1.41 = 1410\text{ g}$.
- **Mass of HNO₃:** $68\%$ of $1410\text{ g} = \mathbf{958.8\text{ g}}$.

**1.7b:**
- **Given:** $40\%\text{ w/w}$, $d = 1.09\text{ g/mL}$.
- **Mass of 500 mL solution:** $500 \times 1.09 = 545\text{ g}$.
- **Mass of HCHO:** $40\%$ of $545\text{ g} = \mathbf{218\text{ g}}$.

**1.7c:**
- **Given:** $98\%\text{ w/w}$, $d = 1.84\text{ g/mL}$. Target = $196\text{ g}$ pure H₂SO₄.
- **Mass of required solution:** $196 / 0.98 = 200\text{ g}$.
- **Volume of solution:** $V = m / d = 200 / 1.84 = \mathbf{108.7\text{ mL}}$.

**1.7d:**
- **Given:** $5.25\%\text{ w/w}$, $d = 1.08\text{ g/mL}$.
- **Mass of 400 mL solution:** $400 \times 1.08 = 432\text{ g}$.
- **Mass of NaOCl:** $5.25\%$ of $432\text{ g} = \mathbf{22.68\text{ g}}$.

**1.7e:**
- **Given:** $28\%\text{ w/w}$, $d = 0.90\text{ g/mL}$. Target = $126\text{ g}$ pure NH₃.
- **Mass of required solution:** $126 / 0.28 = 450\text{ g}$.
- **Volume of solution:** $V = 450 / 0.90 = \mathbf{500\text{ mL}}$.

**1.7f:**
- **Given:** $60\%\text{ w/w}$, $d = 0.85\text{ g/mL}$.
- **Mass of 3 L (3000 mL) solution:** $3000 \times 0.85 = 2550\text{ g}$.
- **Water %:** $100\% - 60\% = 40\%$.
- **Mass of water:** $40\%$ of $2550\text{ g} = \mathbf{1020\text{ g}}$ (or $1.02\text{ kg}$).
</details>

### Type 8: Temperature Independence — Conceptual

**The Pattern:** Why doesn't mass percent change with temperature?

#### Solved Example 1.8
**Q:** Explain why mass percent is temperature-independent, while molarity is temperature-dependent. 🟢

**Solution:**
```
Mass percent uses ONLY mass (grams) in both numerator and denominator:
    w/w% = (mass of solute / mass of solution) × 100

Mass does NOT change when temperature changes — 
heating a solution doesn't create or destroy matter.

Molarity uses VOLUME in the denominator:
    M = moles of solute / volume of solution (in L)

Volume DOES change with temperature — 
liquids expand when heated and contract when cooled.

Therefore:
    - Mass % → Temperature INDEPENDENT ✅
    - Molarity → Temperature DEPENDENT ✅
```

#### Practice Questions — Type 8

| # | Question | Difficulty |
|---|----------|------------|
| 1.8a | A 15% NaCl solution is heated from 25°C to 75°C. Does the mass percent change? Explain. | 🟢 |
| 1.8b | Classify these as temperature-dependent or independent: w/w%, v/v%, molarity, molality, mole fraction, ppm. | 🟡 |
| 1.8c | A student argues: "If I heat a solution, water evaporates, so mass percent changes." Is this argument valid? Under what conditions? | 🔴 |
| 1.8d | A laboratory technician accidentally leaves a 10% (w/w) standard salt solution in a hot room where it undergoes thermal expansion without evaporating. Does the mass percent increase, decrease, or remain the same? | 🟢 |
| 1.8e | If an experiment requires highly precise concentration measurements spanning from 5°C to 80°C, explain whether the researcher should choose mass percent or molarity to express concentration. | 🟡 |
| 1.8f | Compare two solutions prepared at 25°C: 10% (w/w) NaCl and 1.5 M NaCl. If both solutions are stored in a cold room at 4°C, explain the fundamental reason why the stated concentration of one becomes inaccurate while the other remains perfectly true. | 🔴 |

<details>
<summary>💡 Detailed Solutions for Type 8</summary>

**1.8a:**
- **No.** Mass percent depends entirely on masses measured via mass/gravity, which neither increase nor decrease with standard temperature changes unless evaporation/boiling occurs.

**1.8b:**
- **Temperature-Independent:** w/w% (mass/mass), molality (moles/kg mass), mole fraction (moles/moles), ppm (usually mass/mass).
- **Temperature-Dependent:** v/v% (volume/volume), molarity (moles/L volume). Anything involving volume tends to change.

**1.8c:**
- **Valid, but only if evaporation is happening.** If you heat a solution and the solvent actually evaporates, the mass of the solvent drops, decreasing the mass of the solution while solute remains. Thus, the mass percent *increases*. If it's in a completely sealed container where no evaporation escapes, the mass percent remains unchanged regardless of heat.

**1.8d:**
- **Remain the same.** Thermal expansion changes the volume of the solution, but the physical mass of the water and salt in the container remains exactly the same. No evaporation = no mass change.

**1.8e:**
- **Mass percent** (or molality). From 5°C to 80°C, the density/volume of water changes significantly, making volumetric methods like Molarity imprecise across that temperature swing. Mass remains invariant.

**1.8f:**
- Cold temperatures contract liquids, decreasing the solution's volume. Since molarity is defined as moles / volume, a smaller volume means the **molarity (1.5 M) actually increases slightly and becomes inaccurate** to the label. In contrast, 10% w/w is mass/mass. Masses do not contract or expand, so the **10% w/w solution remains perfectly true**.
</details>

| 1.M5 | A solution of urea has mass percent 10%. If someone adds 50 g more water to 200 g of this solution, what is the new mass percent? | T2 + T5 | 🔴 |

<details>
<summary>💡 Detailed Solutions for Type Mixer</summary>

**1.M1:**
- **(a) Mass of NaCl in 500 mL:** Solution mass = $500 \times 1.15 = 575\text{ g}$. Mass of NaCl = $20\%$ of $575 = \mathbf{115\text{ g}}$.
- **(b) Mass of water in 500 mL:** $575 - 115 = \mathbf{460\text{ g}}$.

**1.M2:**
- **Solution mass:** $2\text{ L} (2000\text{ mL}) \times 1.10\text{ g/mL} = 2200\text{ g}$.
- **Mass of H₂SO₄:** $15\%$ of $2200 = \mathbf{330\text{ g}}$.
- **Mass of water:** $2200 - 330 = \mathbf{1870\text{ g}}$.

**1.M3:**
- **Solution mass:** $200\text{ mL} \times 1.19 = 238\text{ g}$.
- **(a) Mass of HCl:** $37\%$ of $238 = \mathbf{88.06\text{ g}}$.
- **(b) Mass of water:** $238 - 88.06 = \mathbf{149.94\text{ g}}$.
- **(c) Mass % of water:** $100\% - 37\% = \mathbf{63\%}$.

**1.M4:**
- **(a) w/w%:** Solute = $40\text{ g}$, Solution = $40+360 = 400\text{ g}$. $\%$ = $(40/400)\times 100 = \mathbf{10\%}$.
- **(b) Volume:** $V = m/d = 400 / 1.11 = \mathbf{360.36\text{ mL}}$.

**1.M5:**
- **Original Solute in 200g:** $10\%$ of $200\text{ g} = 20\text{ g}$.
- **New Solution Mass:** $200\text{ g} + 50\text{ g} = 250\text{ g}$.
- **New Mass %:** $(20 / 250) \times 100 = \mathbf{8\%}$.
</details>

## 📋 Stage 5: Board Arsenal

**NCERT & Board-style questions. Clean, direct. No tricks.**

| # | Question | Difficulty |
|---|----------|------------|
| 1.B1 | A solution is prepared by adding 2 g of a substance A to 18 g of water. Calculate the mass percent of the solute. *(NCERT)* | 🟢 |
| 1.B2 | Calculate the mass percent of benzene (C₆H₆) in solution containing 80 g of benzene in 320 g of carbon tetrachloride (CCl₄). | 🟢 |
| 1.B3 | A solution of glucose has a mass percentage of 36%. Calculate the mass of glucose to be dissolved in 800 g of water to prepare this solution. ⭐ | 🟡 |
| 1.B4 | If 20 mL of ethanol (d = 0.789 g/mL) is dissolved in 80 mL of water (d = 1.0 g/mL), calculate the mass percent of ethanol. | 🟡 |
| 1.B5 | Concentrated nitric acid has a mass percentage of 68% and density 1.41 g/mL. What mass of HNO₃ is present in 500 mL of this acid? | 🟡 |

<details>
<summary>💡 Detailed Solutions for Board Arsenal</summary>

**1.B1:**
- **Given:** Solute = $2\text{ g}$, Solvent = $18\text{ g}$.
- **Solution mass:** $2 + 18 = 20\text{ g}$.
- **Mass %:** $(2 / 20) \times 100 = \mathbf{10\%}$.

**1.B2:**
- **Given:** Solute (benzene) = $80\text{ g}$, Solvent (CCl₄) = $320\text{ g}$.
- **Solution mass:** $80 + 320 = 400\text{ g}$.
- **Mass %:** $(80 / 400) \times 100 = \mathbf{20\%}$.

**1.B3:**
- **Given:** $36\%$ glucose. This means in $100\text{ g}$ solution, there are $36\text{ g}$ glucose and $64\text{ g}$ water.
- **Ratio:** $\frac{\text{Mass of Glucose}}{\text{Mass of Water}} = \frac{36}{64}$.
- **For 800 g water:** $\frac{x}{800} = \frac{36}{64} \rightarrow x = \mathbf{450\text{ g}}$ of glucose. *(Alternatively: $64\% \rightarrow 800\text{ g}$, so $1\% \rightarrow 12.5\text{ g}$, so $36\% \rightarrow 450\text{ g}$)*

**1.B4:**
- **Mass of ethanol:** $20\text{ mL} \times 0.789 = 15.78\text{ g}$.
- **Mass of water:** $80\text{ mL} \times 1.0 = 80\text{ g}$.
- **Solution mass:** $15.78 + 80 = 95.78\text{ g}$.
- **Mass % ethanol:** $(15.78 / 95.78) \times 100 = \mathbf{16.47\%}$.

**1.B5:**
- **Solution mass:** $500\text{ mL} \times 1.41\text{ g/mL} = 705\text{ g}$.
- **Mass of HNO₃:** $68\%$ of $705\text{ g} = 0.68 \times 705 = \mathbf{479.4\text{ g}}$.
</details>

| # | Question | Difficulty |
|---|----------|------------|
| 1.J1 | A 62% (w/w) H₂SO₄ solution has density 1.52 g/mL. Calculate the mass of water in 1.5 L of this solution. | 🟡 |
| 1.J2 | 10 g of glucose is dissolved in 90 g of water. If the density of the solution is 1.02 g/mL, express the concentration in (a) mass percent (b) grams per litre. ⭐ | 🟡 |
| 1.J3 | A solution is prepared by mixing 100 g of 30% NaCl solution with 200 g of 15% NaCl solution. What is the mass percent of the resulting solution? | 🔴 |
| 1.J4 | Commercial sulphuric acid is 98% by mass (d = 1.84 g/mL). If 50 mL of this acid is diluted with water to make 1 L of solution (d = 1.07 g/mL), find the mass percent of the diluted solution. ⭐ | 🔴 |
| 1.J5 | An aqueous solution contains 36% HCl by mass. If 1 mole of HCl weighs 36.5 g and the density of the solution is 1.18 g/mL, find the mass of solution that contains exactly 1 mole of HCl. | 🔴 |

<details>
<summary>💡 Detailed Solutions for JEE Mains Arena</summary>

**1.J1:**
- **Solution mass:** $1.5\text{ L} = 1500\text{ mL} \times 1.52 = 2280\text{ g}$.
- **Mass of water:** The acid is $62\%$ w/w, so water is $38\%$ w/w.
- $(0.38 \times 2280) = \mathbf{866.4\text{ g}}$.

**1.J2:**
- **(a) Mass percent:** Solute = $10\text{ g}$, Solvent = $90\text{ g}$, Solution = $100\text{ g}$. Mass % = $\mathbf{10\%}$.
- **(b) Grams per litre:** Volume of solution = $m/d = 100\text{ g} / 1.02\text{ g/mL} = 98.04\text{ mL} = 0.09804\text{ L}$. Concentration = $10\text{ g} / 0.09804\text{ L} = \mathbf{102\text{ g/L}}$.

**1.J3:**
- **Mass of NaCl in sol 1:** $30\%$ of $100\text{ g} = 30\text{ g}$.
- **Mass of NaCl in sol 2:** $15\%$ of $200\text{ g} = 30\text{ g}$.
- **Total Solute:** $30 + 30 = 60\text{ g}$.
- **Total Solution:** $100 + 200 = 300\text{ g}$.
- **Final Mass %:** $(60 / 300) \times 100 = \mathbf{20\%}$.

**1.J4:**
- **Mass of concentrated acid:** $50\text{ mL} \times 1.84 = 92\text{ g}$.
- **Mass of pure H₂SO₄:** $98\%$ of $92\text{ g} = 90.16\text{ g}$.
- **Mass of final diluted solution:** $1\text{ L} (1000\text{ mL}) \times 1.07 = 1070\text{ g}$.
- **Final Mass %:** $(90.16 / 1070) \times 100 = \mathbf{8.43\%}$.

**1.J5:**
- **We need 1 mole of HCl**, which is $36.5\text{ g}$.
- **Given $36\%\text{ w/w}$:** This means $36\text{ g}$ of HCl is in $100\text{ g}$ of solution.
- **Ratio:** $100\text{ g}$ solution / $36\text{ g}$ HCl = $x\text{ g}$ solution / $36.5\text{ g}$ HCl.
- $x = (100 \times 36.5) / 36 = \mathbf{101.39\text{ g}}$. *(Note: density was given as extra/distracting information since the question asked for mass, not volume!).*
</details>

## Key Takeaways from Chapter 1

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                MASS PERCENT SUMMARY                  │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                      │
│  Formula: w/w% = (W_solute / W_solution) × 100      │
│                                                      │
│  Denominator = SOLUTION (not solvent!)               │
│  W_solution = W_solute + W_solvent                   │
│                                                      │
│  Temperature: INDEPENDENT ✅                         │
│  Units: Dimensionless (%)                            │
│                                                      │
│  To involve volume: Use DENSITY as bridge            │
│     mass = density × volume                          │
│                                                      │
│  Commercial acids: Always given as w/w% + density    │
│                                                      │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```
