# Chapter 6: Molarity (M) — The King of Concentration Terms
## Part II: Enter the Mole

---

## 🎯 Stage 1: The Core Idea

### What is Molarity?

Molarity tells you how many moles of solute are dissolved per litre of **solution** (not solvent!).

> **Analogy:** Imagine a 1-litre bottle of mango juice. Molarity asks: "How many scoops of mango pulp went into this entire bottle?" It doesn't care about the water separately — it looks at the final product.

### Why Molarity is King

| Reason | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Lab standard** | Almost all lab work uses molarity — volumetric analysis, titrations, dilutions |
| **Easy to measure** | Just use a volumetric flask — fill to the mark |
| **Direct stoichiometry** | Moles = M × V(L) — instant access to moles from volume |
| **Most asked in exams** | More than 60% of concentration problems involve molarity |

### The One Weakness

Molarity is **temperature-dependent** ❌. Heating a solution expands the volume → same moles in more volume → molarity decreases. This is why molality is preferred for colligative properties.

---

## 🔬 Stage 2: The Formula Lab

### The Formula

```
       Moles of solute           n
M = ──────────────────────── = ─────
     Volume of solution (L)    V(L)
```

### Expanded Form

```
         W_solute × 1000
M = ──────────────────────────
      M_solute × V_solution(mL)

where:
    W_solute = mass of solute (g)
    M_solute = molar mass of solute (g/mol)
    V_solution = volume of SOLUTION in mL
```

### The Most Useful Rearrangement

```
Moles of solute = M × V(L)

or equivalently:

Millimoles of solute = M × V(mL)
```

> **This is the single most used equation in chemistry lab work.** Every titration, every dilution, every stoichiometric calculation in solution uses this.

### Critical Distinction from Molality

```
MOLARITY: denominator = Volume of SOLUTION (in L)
MOLALITY: denominator = Mass of SOLVENT (in kg)

Volume of SOLUTION ≠ Mass of SOLVENT
```

### Units

```
M = mol/L = mol·L⁻¹ = molar

1 M = 1 mol/L = 1 molar
1 mM = 10⁻³ M = 1 millimolar
```

### Temperature Dependence

Molarity is **temperature-dependent** ❌

Why? Volume changes with temperature (thermal expansion). As T increases, V increases, M decreases. The moles don't change — only the volume does.

### Type 1: Direct — Given mass, molar mass, volume → find M

**The Pattern:** The most basic molarity calculation. All quantities given directly.

#### Solved Example 6.1
**Q:** 4 g of NaOH (M = 40) is dissolved in water to make 500 mL of solution. Find the molarity. 🟢

**Solution:**
```
Step 1: Calculate moles
    n = mass/molar mass = 4/40 = 0.1 mol

Step 2: Convert volume to litres
    V = 500 mL = 0.5 L

Step 3: Calculate molarity
    M = n/V = 0.1/0.5 = 0.2 M

Answer: 0.2 M
```

> **⚠️ Common Mistake:** "Dissolved in 500 mL of water" vs "to make 500 mL of solution." These are DIFFERENT! Molarity uses the volume of the FINAL solution, not the volume of water added.

### Type 2: Reverse — Given M and volume → find mass of solute

**The Pattern:** Molarity and volume are given → how much solute is present?

#### Solved Example 6.2
**Q:** How many grams of HCl (M = 36.5) are present in 200 mL of 0.5 M HCl? 🟢

**Solution:**
```
Step 1: Find moles

Step 2: Convert to mass
    mass = n × molar mass = 0.1 × 36.5 = 3.65 g

Answer: 3.65 g of HCl
```

### Type 3: Reverse — Given M and moles → find volume

**The Pattern:** How much solution volume do you need to get a certain number of moles?

#### Solved Example 6.3
**Q:** What volume of 0.5 M H₂SO₄ contains 0.25 moles of H₂SO₄? 🟢

**Solution:**
```

    V = 0.25/0.5 = 0.5 L = 500 mL

```

### Type 4: Given w/w% + Density → Find Molarity ⭐⭐ (Most Board-JEE Asked Type)

**The Pattern:** This is THE most frequently tested conversion. Mass percent and density are given → find molarity.

#### Derivation
```
Consider 100 g of solution.
    Mass of solute = w/w% = x g
    Moles of solute = x / M_solute
    
    Volume of 100 g solution = mass / density = 100/d mL = 100/(d × 1000) L

    Molarity = moles / volume(L)
             = (x / M_solute) / (100 / (d × 1000))
             = (x × d × 1000) / (M_solute × 100)
             = (x × d × 10) / M_solute
```

#### The Master Formula: w/w% → Molarity

```
         w/w% × d × 10
M = ─────────────────────
          M_solute

where d = density in g/mL
```

> **Memorize differently:** "Percentage times density times ten, divided by molar mass, gives M."

#### Solved Example 6.4
**Q:** Concentrated H₂SO₄ is 98% by mass with density 1.84 g/mL. Find its molarity. (M = 98) 🟡 ⭐⭐

**Solution:**
```
Using the formula:
    M = (w/w% × d × 10) / M_solute
    M = (98 × 1.84 × 10) / 98
    M = (1803.2) / 98
    M = 18.4 M

From first principles (recommended for Board answers):
    Consider 100 g of solution:
        Mass of H₂SO₄ = 98 g
        Moles = 98/98 = 1 mol
        Volume = 100/1.84 = 54.35 mL = 0.05435 L
        M = 1/0.05435 = 18.4 M ✅

Answer: M ≈ 18.4 M
```

> **Why this is tested so much:** Commercial acids (HCl, HNO₃, H₂SO₄) always come with w/w% and density on the label. Converting to molarity is the first step in any lab calculation.

### Type 5: Given w/v% → Find Molarity

**The Pattern:** Weight/volume percent directly gives mass per 100 mL of solution.

#### Relationship
```
w/v% = mass of solute (g) per 100 mL of solution

            w/v%
M = ────────────────────
     M_solute × 0.1

OR equivalently:

         w/v% × 10
M = ─────────────────
        M_solute
```

#### Solved Example 6.5
**Q:** A solution is 5% w/v glucose (M = 180). Find its molarity. 🟢

**Solution:**
```
5% w/v means 5 g of glucose per 100 mL of solution.

    In 1000 mL (1 L): 50 g of glucose
    Moles = 50/180 = 0.278 mol
    M = 0.278 M

Using formula: M = (5 × 10)/180 = 0.278 M ✅

Answer: M ≈ 0.28 M
```

### Type 6: Molarity from Molality + Density ⭐

**The Pattern:** Reverse of the Chapter 5 conversion. Given molality and density → find molarity.

#### The Formula (Reverse of Ch5 T7)

```
              m × d × 1000
M = ──────────────────────────────
       1000 + m × M_solute

where m = molality, d = density (g/mL), M_solute = molar mass
```

#### Solved Example 6.6
**Q:** An aqueous solution of urea (M = 60) has molality = 5 m and density = 1.08 g/mL. Find the molarity. 🔴 ⭐

**Solution:**
```
Step 1: Consider 1 kg (1000 g) of solvent
    Moles of urea = 5 mol (since m = 5)
    Mass of urea = 5 × 60 = 300 g
    
Step 2: Total mass of solution
    mass = 1000 + 300 = 1300 g

Step 3: Volume of solution
    V = mass/d = 1300/1.08 = 1203.7 mL = 1.2037 L

Step 4: Molarity
    M = 5/1.2037 = 4.15 M

Using formula: M = (5 × 1.08 × 1000)/(1000 + 5 × 60) = 5400/1300 = 4.15 ✅

Answer: M ≈ 4.15 M
```

> **Why this works:** Start with "1 kg of solvent" (the natural starting point for molality). Then calculate total mass, use density to find volume, and divide moles by volume.

### Type 11: Why Molarity Changes with Temperature

**The Pattern:** Conceptual understanding.

#### Solved Example 6.11
**Q:** A 1 M NaCl solution is prepared at 25°C. The solution is then heated to 50°C. Will the molarity increase, decrease, or stay the same? Explain. 🟡

**Solution:**
```
When heated from 25°C to 50°C:
    - Moles of NaCl remain CONSTANT (heating doesn't destroy NaCl)
    - Volume of solution INCREASES (thermal expansion of liquid)
    
    Since M = n/V, and n is constant but V increases:
    → M DECREASES

Answer: Molarity decreases.

Quantitative estimate:
    Water's coefficient of thermal expansion ≈ 2.1 × 10⁻⁴ /°C
    ΔT = 25°C
    ΔV/V ≈ 2.1 × 10⁻⁴ × 25 = 0.00525 = 0.525%
    
    So molarity decreases by about 0.5% — small but measurable.
```

> **The thermometer analogy:** Molarity is like a measuring tape that stretches when heated. The thing being measured (moles) hasn't changed, but the ruler has expanded, giving a smaller reading.

*   **Reasoning:** $M = n/V$. At 4°C, density is maximum, meaning volume $V$ is at its **minimum**. Since volume is in the denominator, a minimum volume gives a **maximum** molarity.
*   **Answer:** Molarity will be maximum at 4°C.

**6.11b: Why doesn't this problem exist for molality, mass %, or mole fraction?**
*   **Reasoning:** All these terms rely exclusively on **mass** (mass of solute, mass of solvent). Mass is highly conserved and does not change with temperature fluctuations, unlike volume which undergoes thermal expansion/contraction.

**6.11c: 0.5 M KCl prepared at 20°C is moved to 35°C. Why does M decrease?**
*   **Reasoning:** At higher temperatures, the thermal kinetic energy causes the solution to expand (volume increases). The number of moles of KCl remains absolutely fixed. Since $M = n/V$ and $V$ has increased, the calculated value of $M$ must decrease.

**6.11d: 2.0 M sugar solution warms from 5°C to 25°C. Does expansion alter moles or just read-out?**
*   **Reasoning:** The expansion only alters the volume and thus the concentration "read-out" (molarity per litre). The total amount (moles) of sugar in the flask is physically unchanged.

**6.11e: Errors in standard analytical concentrations due to temp fluctuations.**
*   **Reasoning:** A volumetric flask holds exactly its marked volume only at its calibration temperature (usually 20°C). If the lab is at 30°C, the solution inside expands past the calibration mark when standardising. The defined molarity will be artificially distorted, creating systematic errors in all subsequent titration/analytical work during different seasons.

</details>

### Type 12: Molarity and Stoichiometry (Reaction-Based)

**The Pattern:** Using molarity in balanced chemical equations.

#### Solved Example 6.12
**Q:** 25 mL of 0.1 M NaOH is mixed with 25 mL of 0.1 M HCl. The reaction is:
NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H₂O
Find: (a) moles of NaOH and HCl (b) which reagent is in excess? (c) moles of NaCl formed. 🟡

**Solution:**
```
Step 1: Find moles (using mmol for convenience)
    mmol NaOH = 0.1 × 25 = 2.5 mmol
    mmol HCl = 0.1 × 25 = 2.5 mmol

Step 2: Stoichiometry
    Reaction: NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H₂O  (1:1 ratio)
    NaOH : HCl = 2.5 : 2.5 = 1 : 1
    
    Both are in stoichiometric amounts — neither is in excess.

Step 3: Products
    mmol NaCl formed = 2.5 mmol (stoichiometric)
    moles NaCl = 0.0025 mol

Answer: (a) 2.5 mmol each  (b) Neither — exact stoichiometric match
        (c) 2.5 mmol = 0.0025 mol NaCl
```

#### Solved Example 6.12B
**Q:** 30 mL of 0.5 M H₂SO₄ reacts with excess NaOH. How many moles of water are formed?
H₂SO₄ + 2NaOH → Na₂SO₄ + 2H₂O 🟡 ⭐

**Solution:**
```
    mmol H₂SO₄ = 0.5 × 30 = 15 mmol

    From stoichiometry: 1 mol H₂SO₄ → 2 mol H₂O
    mmol H₂O = 15 × 2 = 30 mmol = 0.03 mol

Answer: 0.03 mol of water
```

**6.12d: 100 mL 1 M HCl + 50 mL 1 M NaOH. Leftover HCl? Final [HCl]?**

    2.  mmol NaOH available $= 50 \times 1 = 50\text{ mmol}$
    3.  NaOH is limiting. Excess HCl $= 100 - 50 = 50\text{ mmol}$
    4.  Total Volume $= 100 + 50 = 150\text{ mL}$
    5.  Final $[\text{HCl}] = 50\text{ mmol} / 150\text{ mL} = 0.333\text{ M}$

<summary>💡 Detailed Solutions for Type Mixer</summary>

**6.M1: Concentrated H₂SO₄ (98%, d=1.84, M=98). Find interconversions.**
*   **Calculation:**
    1.  **Molarity:** $M = \frac{98 \times 1.84 \times 10}{98} = 18.4\text{ M}$
    2.  **Molality:** Mass of solute = $98\text{ g}$, solvent (water) = $2\text{ g}$.
        $m = \frac{98/98}{2/1000} = \frac{1}{0.002} = 500\text{ m}$
    3.  **Mole Fraction:** Moles $\text{H}_2\text{O} = \frac{2}{18} = 0.111\text{ mol}$.

**6.M2: 5 M NaCl (d = 1.15 g/mL, M = 58.5). Find interconversions.**
*   **Calculation:**
    1.  **w/w%:** $M = \frac{w \times d \times 10}{M} \Rightarrow 5 = \frac{w \times 1.15 \times 10}{58.5} \Rightarrow w = 25.43\%$
    2.  **Molality:** Assume $1\text{ L} = 1000\text{ mL}$. Mass of solution $= 1150\text{ g}$.
        Moles = $5$. Mass of solute $= 5 \times 58.5 = 292.5\text{ g}$.
        Mass of solvent $= 1150 - 292.5 = 857.5\text{ g} = 0.8575\text{ kg}$.
        $m = \frac{5}{0.8575} = 5.83\text{ m}$
    3.  **Mole Fraction:** Moles $\text{H}_2\text{O} = \frac{857.5}{18} = 47.64\text{ mol}$.
        $\chi_{\text{NaCl}} = \frac{5}{5 + 47.64} = 0.095$
*   **Answer:** (a) $25.43\%$ (b) $5.83\text{ m}$ (c) $0.095$

**6.M3: 25 mL 0.2 M AgNO₃ + 25 mL 0.1 M CaCl₂.**

    2.  $\text{CaCl}_2 \rightarrow \text{Ca}^{2+} + 2\text{Cl}^-$. $\text{mmol}\text{ CaCl}_2 = 25 \times 0.1 = 2.5\text{ mmol}$.
        $\text{mmol}\text{ Cl}^- = 2 \times 2.5 = 5\text{ mmol}$.
    3.  Reaction: $\text{Ag}^+ + \text{Cl}^- \rightarrow \text{AgCl}\downarrow$.
        $5\text{ mmol}\text{ Ag}^+$ reacts with $5\text{ mmol}\text{ Cl}^-$. Exact match, neither is in excess.
    4.  $\text{mmol}\text{ AgCl}\text{ formed} = 5\text{ mmol} = 0.005\text{ mol}$.
    5.  Final $[\text{Ag}^+] \approx 0$, $[\text{Cl}^-] \approx 0$ (assumed complete precipitation).
*   **Answer:** (a) $5\text{ mmol}$ each (b) Yes (c) $0.005\text{ mol}$ (d) zero (technically determined by $K_{sp}$)
