# Chapter 2: Conductors, Insulators & Charging by Induction

> *NCERT Sections 1.3–1.4*

## 🎯 Stage 1: The Core Idea

### Conductors vs Insulators — The Highway vs the Wall

Imagine charge as water.

- A **conductor** is a pipe — water flows freely through it. Metals like copper, silver, aluminium are conductors. They have **free electrons** (also called conduction electrons) that can move throughout the material.
- An **insulator** (dielectric) is a sealed bottle — water is trapped inside and can't flow. Materials like rubber, glass, wood, plastic are insulators. Their electrons are tightly bound to atoms.

| Property | Conductor | Insulator |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Free electrons? | Yes — ~10²⁸ per m³ | No (electrons bound to atoms) |
| Charge flows through? | Yes, easily | No |
| Charge stays where placed? | No — distributes over surface | Yes — stays put |
| Examples | Metals, human body, Earth | Glass, rubber, mica, wood |

> 💡 **Semiconductors** (silicon, germanium) are in-between — their conductivity can be controlled. Not relevant for this chapter, but crucial in Chapter 14.

### Three Methods of Charging

| Method | Requires Contact? | Charge Type | Key Idea |
|--------|:-----------------:|:-----------:|----------|
| **Friction (rubbing)** | Yes | Opposite on each body | Electrons transfer at contact surface |
| **Conduction (touching)** | Yes | Same as source | Free electrons flow from charged → neutral |
| **Induction** | **No** | **Opposite** to source | Charges in neutral body rearrange due to nearby charge |

### Charging by Induction — The 4-Step Process ⭐

This is the most exam-important method. Here's exactly how it works:

**Setup:** A positively charged rod is brought near a neutral metal sphere.

1. **Bring charged rod near** (but don't touch) → electrons in sphere migrate toward the rod, leaving the far side positive. The sphere is still neutral overall.

2. **Ground the sphere** (connect to Earth with a wire or touch with finger) → electrons flow in from the ground to neutralize the positive far side. The near side still has excess electrons.

3. **Remove the ground connection** (disconnect wire / lift finger) → the sphere is now trapped with excess electrons. Still near the rod.

4. **Remove the rod** → excess electrons redistribute evenly over the sphere. The sphere now has a **net negative charge** — opposite to the rod.

> 🔑 **Key takeaway:** In induction, the induced charge is always **opposite** to the inducing charge. No material is transferred between the rod and sphere.

### Electroscope

A **gold-leaf electroscope** detects charge:
- Two thin gold leaves are attached to a metal rod inside a glass jar.
- When charge is given to the knob, both leaves acquire the same charge → they repel and diverge.
- Greater charge = greater divergence.

## 🔬 Stage 2: The Formula Lab

There are **no new formulas** in this section — it's conceptual. But you must know:

### Grounding Equation (Conceptual)

When a conductor is grounded:

$$V_{conductor} = 0 \quad \text{(potential becomes zero = Earth's potential)}$$

This means charge flows to/from Earth until the conductor reaches zero potential. The charge that flows equals whatever is needed to make V = 0.

### Charge Distribution on Conductors

For an isolated conductor:
- All excess charge resides on the **outer surface**.
- Electric field **inside** a conductor = 0 (in electrostatic equilibrium).
- Charge density is highest at **sharp points** (this causes corona discharge).

### Type 1: Identify conductor or insulator ⭐

**Pattern:** "Classify the given materials."

**Solved Example** 🟢

> Classify: Copper wire, glass rod, human body, rubber shoes, salt solution, diamond.

**Solution:**
| Material | Type | Reason |
|----------|------|--------|
| Copper wire | Conductor | Metal — free electrons |
| Glass rod | Insulator | No free charge carriers |
| Human body | Conductor | Contains ions and water (electrolyte) |
| Rubber shoes | Insulator | No free electrons |
| Salt solution | Conductor | Free ions (Na⁺, Cl⁻) |
| Diamond | Insulator | All electrons in covalent bonds |

3. 🟡 Is pure silicon a conductor or insulator? Why is it called a semiconductor?

### Type 2: Predict the charge after induction ⭐

**Pattern:** "A charged rod is brought near a neutral conductor. What charge appears on each side?"

**Solved Example** 🟢

> A negatively charged rod is brought near a neutral metal sphere without touching. What charges appear on the near and far side of the sphere?

**Solution:**
- The negative rod **repels** free electrons in the sphere away from itself.
- Near side: loses electrons → **positive** charge appears.
- Far side: gains electrons → **negative** charge appears.
- Net charge on sphere: **still zero** (no contact, no grounding).

**Practice:**

1. 🟢 A positively charged glass rod is brought near a neutral aluminium can. What charges appear on the near and far surfaces? *(Ans: near = negative, far = positive)*
2. 🟡 In the above, if the can is grounded while the rod is near, and then the ground is removed before the rod is removed, what is the final charge on the can? *(Ans: negative)*
3. 🟡 What happens if you remove the rod *before* removing the ground? *(Ans: no net charge — electrons that came from ground return to ground)*

### Type 3: Sequence of operations in induction ⭐⭐

**Pattern:** "Describe what happens at each step of the induction process."

**Solved Example** 🟡

> Describe step-by-step how to charge a metal sphere positively by induction using a negatively charged rod.

**Solution:**

| Step | Action | What happens inside the sphere |
|------|--------|-------------------------------|
| 1 | Bring −ve rod near sphere | Electrons repelled to far side. Near side becomes +ve. |
| 2 | Ground the sphere (touch with finger) | Excess electrons on far side flow to Earth. |
| 3 | Remove ground (lift finger) | Sphere is now deficit in electrons → net +ve charge. |
| 4 | Remove the rod | Positive charge distributes uniformly on surface. |

**Final charge: Positive** ✓ (opposite of the rod — which was negative)

**Practice:**

1. 🟡 How would you charge a sphere negatively using a positive rod by induction? Describe all steps.
2. 🟡 Can you charge an insulator by induction? Explain why or why not. *(Ans: No — free charges cannot redistribute in an insulator)*
3. 🔴 Two metal spheres A and B are placed touching each other. A positive rod is brought near A (not touching). Then A and B are separated, and the rod is removed. What is the charge on each sphere?

<details>
<summary><b>Answer to Q3</b></summary>

When the positive rod is near A:
- Electrons in the A–B system migrate toward A (near the rod).
- A becomes negative, B becomes positive.

When separated (while rod is still near):
- A retains its negative charge.
- B retains its positive charge.

After rod removed: **A is negative, B is positive.**
</details>

### Type 4: Why does a charged rod attract neutral pieces of paper? ⭐

**Pattern:** Conceptual explanation of attraction between charged and neutral bodies.

**Solved Example** 🟢

> A charged comb attracts small pieces of paper even though the paper is neutral. Explain.

**Solution:**

The charged comb **induces** charges in the paper. The molecules in the paper get polarized — the side closer to the comb acquires opposite charge (attracted), the far side acquires same charge (repelled). But the near side is **closer** → force of attraction > force of repulsion → **net attraction**.

> This is the same reason a charged balloon sticks to a wall.

**Practice:**

1. 🟢 Why does a charged rod attract both conductors and insulators?
2. 🟡 A negatively charged balloon sticks to a neutral wall. Explain the mechanism. Is the wall a conductor or insulator? Does it matter?
3. 🟡 If you bring a charged rod near a suspended pith ball, it first gets attracted and then repelled after touching the rod. Explain.

<details>
<summary><b>Answer to Q3</b></summary>

**Phase 1 (Attraction):** The neutral pith ball is attracted by induction (as explained above).

**Phase 2 (Contact):** When it touches the rod, charge is transferred to the pith ball by conduction. Now both the rod and ball carry the same sign of charge.

**Phase 3 (Repulsion):** Like charges repel → ball flies away.
</details>

### Type 5: Electroscope-based problems

**Solved Example** 🟡

> A positively charged rod is brought near the disc of an uncharged electroscope without touching. What happens to the gold leaves? What happens when the rod is removed?

**Solution:**
- Rod near disc → induces negative charge on disc (near side), positive on leaves.
- Both leaves get positive charge → **leaves diverge**.
- Rod removed → induced charges recombine → leaves **collapse back** (no net charge was transferred).

**Practice:**

1. 🟢 If the rod touches the disc, what happens? *(Ans: leaves diverge permanently — charge transferred by conduction)*
2. 🟡 The disc is grounded while the positive rod is near. Ground is removed, then rod is removed. What charge do the leaves have? *(Ans: negative — leaves diverge)*
3. 🟡 How can you use an electroscope to determine the sign of an unknown charge?

### Type 6: Can you charge a conductor/insulator by these methods?

**Solved Example** 🟡

> Can an insulator be charged by (a) friction, (b) conduction, (c) induction?

**Solution:**
- (a) **Friction: Yes** — electrons transfer at contact. This is how you charge rubber, ebonite, etc.
- (b) **Conduction: Poorly** — charge doesn't spread through an insulator, so only the point of contact gets charged.
- (c) **Induction: No** — free charges cannot rearrange in an insulator (they're bound). Polarization occurs at the molecular level, but no net charge can be separated.

**Practice:**

1. 🟢 Why can metals not be easily charged by friction? *(Ans: charges flow away quickly through the body/ground)*
2. 🟡 You rub a metal rod with silk while holding it with bare hands. Will the rod get charged? What if you hold it with a rubber glove?

## 🔀 Stage 4: Type Mixer

**Q1.** 🟡 ⭐ A metal sphere is given a charge of +Q. It is then brought near an uncharged metal sphere and they are connected by a wire. Describe the charge distribution after the wire is removed.

<details>
<summary><b>Solution</b></summary>

When connected by wire: charge flows from the charged sphere to the uncharged one until both reach the same potential. For identical spheres, each gets +Q/2.

After wire removed: Each sphere has +Q/2, distributed on outer surfaces.
</details>

**Q2.** 🔴 Two metal spheres on insulating stands are placed touching each other. A negative rod is brought near sphere A (left side). While the rod is in place:
(a) What are the charges on A and B?
(b) A wire connects B to Earth. What happens?
(c) The wire is removed, then the spheres are separated, then the rod is removed. Final charges?

<details>
<summary><b>Solution</b></summary>

(a) A: positive (electrons attracted toward rod), B: negative (excess electrons pushed to far side). Net system charge = 0.

(b) The negative charge on B flows to Earth (Earth acts as infinite sink). Now: A is positive, B is neutral-ish, but the system lost negative charge. Net system charge = positive.

(c) After wire removed → B has no excess charge. Spheres separated → A keeps its positive charge. Rod removed → A distributes its positive charge uniformly. **A is positive, B is neutral.**
</details>

**Q3.** 🟡 Explain why a Van de Graaff generator works on the principle of induction and corona discharge.

<details>
<summary><b>Solution</b></summary>

The Van de Graaff generator uses:
1. **Corona discharge** at the lower comb (sharp points create high field → charge sprays onto belt)
2. **Induction** at the upper comb (belt carries charge near the comb → induces opposite charge on the dome)
3. Charge accumulates on the **outer surface** of the large dome (conductor property)
4. The large radius means the dome can hold enormous charge before breakdown.
</details>

## 📋 Stage 5: Board Arsenal

**Q1.** 🟢 ⭐ Distinguish between conductors and insulators. Give two examples of each. *(2 marks)*

<details>
<summary><b>Model Answer</b></summary>

| Property | Conductors | Insulators |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Free charge carriers | Have free electrons/ions | No free charge carriers |
| Allow charge flow? | Yes | No |
| Examples | Copper, aluminium | Glass, rubber |
</details>

**Q2.** 🟡 ⭐ Explain the process of charging a metal sphere by induction. Draw diagrams. *(3 marks)*

<details>
<summary><b>Model Answer</b></summary>

**Step 1:** Bring a positively charged rod near (not touching) a neutral metal sphere. Electrons in the sphere are attracted toward the rod and accumulate on the near side. The far side becomes positive.

**Step 2:** While the rod is still near, ground the sphere (connect to Earth). Electrons flow from the ground into the sphere to neutralize the positive far side.

**Step 3:** Remove the ground connection. The sphere now has excess electrons (net negative charge).

**Step 4:** Remove the rod. The negative charge distributes uniformly on the sphere's surface.

**Result:** The sphere acquires a charge **opposite** to the inducing charge, without any direct contact.
</details>

**Q3.** 🟡 A charged rod attracts bits of uncharged paper. Explain why. *(2 marks)*

<details>
<summary><b>Model Answer</b></summary>

When the charged rod is brought near uncharged paper, it polarizes the paper molecules — inducing opposite charge on the near surface and similar charge on the far surface. Since the opposite charge is closer to the rod, the attractive force exceeds the repulsive force, resulting in a net attraction.
</details>

**Q4.** 🟢 Why must the grounding step come *before* removing the rod in charging by induction? *(2 marks)*

<details>
<summary><b>Model Answer</b></summary>

If the rod is removed before grounding, the separated charges (induced by the rod) would recombine — the sphere would return to its neutral state and no net charge would remain. Grounding while the rod is present allows excess charge to flow to/from Earth, and removing the ground first *traps* this charge on the sphere.
</details>

## 🚀 Stage 6: JEE Mains Arena

**Q1.** 🟡 ⭐ A positively charged glass rod is brought near an isolated metallic sphere. The correct statement is:

(a) The sphere gets positively charged  
(b) The near side becomes positive  
(c) The sphere remains neutral but gets polarized  
(d) The sphere gets negatively charged  

<details>
<summary><b>Answer</b></summary>

**(c)** — No charge is transferred (no contact, no grounding). The sphere remains neutral but with induced charge separation (polarization).
</details>

**Q2.** 🟡 Two metallic spheres A and B of identical size are placed in contact. A positively charged rod is brought near A. While the rod is near:

(a) Both A and B are positive  
(b) A is negative, B is positive  
(c) A is positive, B is negative  
(d) Both are neutral  

<details>
<summary><b>Answer</b></summary>

**(b)** — Electrons in the combined conductor are attracted toward the positive rod, making A negative and B (far side) positive.
</details>

**Q3.** 🔴 A positively charged ball is brought near an isolated conductor. The conductor is then grounded while the ball is nearby. The ground is removed, and then the ball is removed. The conductor will have:

(a) No charge  
(b) Positive charge  
(c) Negative charge  
(d) First negative, then positive as the ball is removed  

<details>
<summary><b>Answer</b></summary>

**(c)** — Grounding while the positive ball is near allows electrons to flow in from Earth (attracted by the nearby positive charge). After removing ground and then ball, the conductor retains excess electrons → **negative charge**.
</details>

**Q4.** 🔴 ⭐ A charge Q is placed at the centre of a metallic spherical shell. The charge on the inner and outer surfaces of the shell are:

(a) −Q and +Q  
(b) +Q and −Q  
(c) 0 and Q  
(d) Q and 0  

<details>
<summary><b>Answer</b></summary>

**(a)** — By induction, the inner surface gets −Q (to make E = 0 inside the metal) and the outer surface gets +Q (to conserve total charge of the shell, which was zero).
</details>
