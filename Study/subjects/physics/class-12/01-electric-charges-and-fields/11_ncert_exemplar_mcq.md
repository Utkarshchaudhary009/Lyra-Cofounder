## MCQ Type I (Single Correct Answer)

### Q 1.1 ⭐

In the figure, two positive charges q₂ and q₃ fixed along the y-axis, exert a net electric force in the +x direction on a charge q₁ fixed along the x-axis. If a positive charge Q is added at (x, 0), the force on q₁:

(a) shall increase along the positive x-axis
(b) shall decrease along the positive x-axis
(c) shall point along the negative x-axis
(d) shall increase but the direction changes because of the interaction of Q with q₂ and q₃

<details>
<summary><b>Answer & Solution</b></summary>

**Answer: (a)**

The charge Q at (x, 0) is on the positive x-axis. Since Q is positive and q₁ is also on the x-axis with net force already in +x direction, the new charge Q will exert an additional force on q₁ along the +x direction (repulsion pushes q₁ further in +x). The principle of superposition tells us that the force due to Q is independent of the forces from q₂ and q₃. So the total force increases along +x.
</details>

### Q 1.2

A point positive charge is brought near an isolated conducting sphere. The electric field is best given by:

*(Figures show 4 different field line patterns around a sphere near a point charge)*

(a) Fig (i) — field lines curve and are perpendicular to the sphere surface, with induced charge separation shown
(b) Fig (ii)
(c) Fig (iii)
(d) Fig (iv)

<details>
<summary><b>Answer & Solution</b></summary>

**Answer: (a)**

When a positive charge is brought near an isolated conducting sphere:
- Negative charge is induced on the near side, positive on the far side
- Field lines must be **perpendicular** to the conductor surface (property of conductors)
- Inside the conductor, E = 0
- The field line pattern shows distortion due to induced charges

Only Fig (i) correctly shows all these features.
</details>

### Q 1.3 ⭐

The electric flux through the surface:

*(Figures show the same charge enclosed by surfaces of different shapes — sphere, cube, irregular shape, elongated shape)*

(a) in Fig (iv) is the largest
(b) in Fig (iii) is the least
(c) in Fig (ii) is same as Fig (iii) but is smaller than Fig (iv)
(d) is the same for all the figures

<details>
<summary><b>Answer & Solution</b></summary>

**Answer: (d)**

By **Gauss's Law**, the total electric flux through any closed surface depends ONLY on the charge enclosed, not on the shape or size of the surface.

Φ = q_enclosed / ε₀

Since all four surfaces enclose the same charge, the flux is identical for all.

> 🔑 This is one of the most important conceptual results of Gauss's law.
</details>

### Q 1.4 ⭐⭐

Five charges q₁, q₂, q₃, q₄, and q₅ are fixed at their positions as shown in Fig 1.4. S is a Gaussian surface. The Gauss's law is given by ∮ E⃗ · dS⃗ = q/ε₀. Which of the following statements is correct?

(a) E on the LHS will have a contribution from q₁, q₅ and q₃ while q on the RHS will have a contribution from q₂ and q₄ only
(b) E on the LHS will have a contribution from all charges while q on the RHS will have a contribution from q₂ and q₄ only
(c) E on the LHS will have a contribution from all charges while q on the RHS will have a contribution from q₁, q₃ and q₅ only
(d) Both E on the LHS and q on the RHS will have contributions from q₂ and q₄ only

<details>
<summary><b>Answer & Solution</b></summary>

**Answer: (b)**

This is a **critical conceptual distinction** in Gauss's law:

- **LHS (∮ E⃗ · dS⃗):** The electric field E at any point on the Gaussian surface is due to **ALL charges** — both inside and outside the surface. External charges affect the local field at every point.

- **RHS (q/ε₀):** The charge q is ONLY the **net charge enclosed** within the Gaussian surface. External charges contribute zero net flux (whatever flux enters also exits).

If q₂ and q₄ are inside surface S, then q = q₂ + q₄ on the RHS, but E on the LHS includes contributions from all five charges.

> 🔑 This is the #1 misconception about Gauss's law.
</details>

### Q 1.5 ⭐

Figure shows electric field lines in which an electric dipole p⃗ is placed as shown. Which of the following statements is correct?

*(Field lines are converging from left to right, i.e., the field is non-uniform and stronger on the left)*

(a) The dipole will not experience any force
(b) The dipole will experience a force towards right
(c) The dipole will experience a force towards left
(d) The dipole will experience a force upwards

<details>
<summary><b>Answer & Solution</b></summary>

**Answer: (c)**

The field lines are **converging** → the field is **non-uniform** (stronger where lines are denser = left side).

In a non-uniform field, a dipole experiences a net force toward the region of **stronger field**.

Since the field is stronger on the left, the dipole experiences a force **towards left**.

> Key formula: F = p(dE/dx) — the dipole moves toward stronger field.
</details>

### Q 1.6

A point charge +q is placed at a distance d from an isolated conducting plane. The field at a point P on the other side of the plane is:

(a) directed perpendicular to the plane and away from the plane
(b) directed perpendicular to the plane but towards the plane
(c) directed radially away from the point charge
(d) directed radially towards the point charge

<details>
<summary><b>Answer & Solution</b></summary>

**Answer: (a)**

The conducting plane has induced charges on its surface. On the far side, the induced positive charges create a field directed **perpendicular to the plane and away from it**.

The field is NOT radially from the original charge — the conductor reshapes the field pattern.
</details>

### Q 1.7 ⭐⭐

A positive charge Q is uniformly distributed along a circular ring of radius R. A small test charge q is placed at the centre of the ring. Then:

(a) If q > 0 and is displaced away from the centre in the plane of the ring, it will be pushed back towards the centre
(b) If q < 0 and is displaced away from the centre in the plane of the ring, it will never return to the centre and will continue moving till it hits the ring
(c) If q < 0, it will perform SHM for small displacement along the axis
(d) q at the centre of the ring is in an unstable equilibrium within the plane of the ring for q > 0

<details>
<summary><b>Answer & Solution</b></summary>

**Answer: (a)**

At the centre, E = 0 by symmetry. For displacement **in the plane** of the ring:

If q > 0 is displaced toward one part of the ring, the closer part repels more strongly than the far part → **net restoring force toward centre** → stable equilibrium in the plane.

For q > 0 along the **axis**: force pushes away → unstable.

For q < 0: exactly opposite — stable along axis (SHM), unstable in the plane.

> 🔑 Earnshaw's theorem: Can't be stable in ALL directions simultaneously.
</details>

### Q 1.8 ⭐

If ∮ E⃗ · dS⃗ = 0 over a surface, then:

(a) the electric field inside the surface is necessarily uniform
(b) the electric field inside the surface is necessarily zero
(c) the number of flux lines entering the surface must be equal to the number of flux lines leaving it
(d) the charge inside the surface is zero

<details>
<summary><b>Answer & Solution</b></summary>

**Answer: (c) and (d)**

- **(d)** ✓ q_enclosed = ε₀ × 0 = 0
- **(c)** ✓ Zero net flux → flux in = flux out
- **(a)** ✗ A dipole inside gives zero flux but highly non-uniform E
- **(b)** ✗ Zero flux ≠ zero field everywhere
</details>

### Q 1.9

The electric field at a point is:

(a) always continuous
(b) continuous if there is no charge at that point
(c) discontinuous only if there is a negative charge at that point
(d) discontinuous if there is a charge at that point

<details>
<summary><b>Answer & Solution</b></summary>

**Answer: (b) and (d)**

- **(b)** ✓ In charge-free regions, E is continuous
- **(d)** ✓ At the location of a point charge, E → ∞ (discontinuous). At surface charges, E jumps by σ/ε₀.
- **(c)** ✗ Discontinuity is not limited to negative charges only
</details>

### Q 1.10

If there were only one type of charge in the universe, then:

(a) ∮ E⃗ · dS⃗ ≠ 0 on any surface
(b) ∮ E⃗ · dS⃗ = 0 if the charge is outside the surface
(c) ∮ E⃗ · dS⃗ could not be defined
(d) ∮ E⃗ · dS⃗ = q/ε₀ if charge of magnitude q is inside the surface

<details>
<summary><b>Answer & Solution</b></summary>

**Answer: (b) and (d)**

Gauss's law is a mathematical consequence of the inverse-square nature of Coulomb's law. It does NOT depend on the existence of two types of charges.

- **(b)** ✓ External charge → zero net flux (unchanged)
- **(d)** ✓ Enclosed charge q → flux = q/ε₀ (Gauss's law still holds)
- **(a)** ✗ An empty surface still has zero flux
- **(c)** ✗ Flux is well-defined regardless of charge types
</details>

### Q 1.11

Consider a region inside which there are various types of charges but the total charge is zero. At points outside the region:

(a) the electric field is necessarily zero
(b) the electric field is due to the dipole moment of the charge distribution only
(c) the dominant electric field is ∝ 1/r³ for large r
(d) the electric field is zero at all points

<details>
<summary><b>Answer & Solution</b></summary>

**Answer: (c)**

When total charge = 0, the monopole term (∝ 1/r²) vanishes. The next dominant term in the **multipole expansion** is the dipole term (∝ 1/r³).

- **(c)** ✓ Dominant term at large r falls as 1/r³
- **(b)** ✗ Not ONLY dipole — higher multipoles also contribute, dipole just dominates at large r
- **(a), (d)** ✗ A dipole has zero net charge but non-zero field
</details>

Refer to the arrangement of charges in the figure and a Gaussian surface of radius R centred at the origin. Then:

(a) total flux through the sphere is (−Q)/ε₀
(b) the field on the surface is due to all charges
(c) flux through the surface is due to charges outside only
(d) the field on the surface will only depend on Q

<details>
<summary><b>Answer & Solution</b></summary>

**Answer: (a) and (b)**

</details>

### Q 1.13 ⭐

A positive charge Q is uniformly distributed along a circular ring of radius R. A small test charge q is placed at the centre of the ring. Then:

(a) if q > 0 and is displaced along the axis, it will experience a restoring force
(b) if q < 0 and is displaced along the axis, it will experience a restoring force
(c) if q < 0 and displaced in the plane of the ring, it will experience a restoring force
(d) the equilibrium is always unstable for any sign of q and any direction

<details>
<summary><b>Answer & Solution</b></summary>

**Answer: (b)**

E along axis of ring: E = kQx/(R² + x²)^(3/2) — directed away from centre for x > 0.

- **q < 0 along axis**: F = qE = (negative)(positive) → force toward centre → **restoring** ✓
- **q > 0 along axis**: Force away from centre → not restoring ✗
- **q < 0 in plane**: Force away from centre (toward nearest ring charge) → not restoring ✗

> Earnshaw's theorem: can't be stable in ALL directions, but CAN be stable in one.
</details>
