# 1.1 & 1.2: Introduction and Electric Charge (Quantisation & Properties)

## ?? Stage 1: The Core Idea

### The Analogy: Buying Apples vs. Buying Water
Imagine you go to a store to buy apples. You can buy 1 apple, 2 apples, or 100 apples. But you cannot buy 1.37 apples. Apples are **quantised**—they exist in discrete, countable packets. 

Now, compare this to buying water from a tap. You can pour exactly 1.37 liters of water. Water *appears* continuous.

**Electric charge is like apples, not water.**
Every object in the universe gets its charge from electrons or protons. You can add or remove 1 electron, 2 electrons, or a trillion electrons. But you can never move *half* an electron. 

Therefore, any charge ($q$) on any object in the universe must be an exact integer multiple of the basic charge of one electron ($e$). This is the **Quantisation of Charge**.

**The Three Core Properties of Charge:**
1.  **Quantisation:** Charge exists in discrete packets ($q = \pm ne$).
2.  **Conservation:** The total charge of an isolated system never changes. You can move electrons from a glass rod to silk, but the total number of electrons in the universe stays the same.
3.  **Additivity:** Total charge is the simple algebraic sum of all individual charges (taking signs into account). If a box has +3C, -2C, and +4C, the total charge is +5C.

## ?? Stage 2: The Formula Lab

### The Master Formula: $q = \pm ne$

Let's break down the only formula you need for this section:

| Variable | What it means | Unit | Important Note |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **$q$** | Total charge on the body | Coulombs (C) | Can be positive (lost electrons) or negative (gained electrons). |
| **$n$** | Number of electrons transferred | No unit | **MUST BE AN INTEGER** (1, 2, 3...). If a math problem gives you $n=2.5$, the charge is physically impossible. |
| **$e$** | Elementary charge | Coulombs (C) | The magnitude of charge on one electron/proton. **$e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$**. Memorize this! |

**Sub-Concept: Mass Transfer Formula**
Electrons have mass ($m_e = 9.1 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$). If $n$ electrons move, mass also moves!
$\Delta M = n \times m_e$
*(If an object becomes negatively charged, its mass slightly INCREASES. If positive, it DECREASES).*

## ?? Stage 3: Type-wise Mastery

### Type 1: Finding number of electrons given the charge
**The Pattern:** They give you $q$, you find $n$.
**Example:** How many electrons make up 1 Coulomb of negative charge?
*Solution:* $q = 1 \text{ C}$, $e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$. $n = q/e = 1 / (1.6 \times 10^{-19}) = 6.25 \times 10^{18}$ electrons.

> **Practice 1:** ?? **Easy** | A metal sphere has a positive charge of $3.2 \times 10^{-7} \text{ C}$. How many electrons were removed?
<details>
<summary>View Solution</summary>
$q = 3.2 \times 10^{-7} \text{ C}$
$n = \frac{q}{e} = \frac{3.2 \times 10^{-7}}{1.6 \times 10^{-19}}$
$n = 2 \times 10^{12}$ electrons.
</details>

### Type 2: "Is this charge possible?" (The Trap Question)
**The Pattern:** You calculate $n$. If $n$ has a decimal, you answer "NO".
**Example:** Can a body have a charge of $0.8 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$?
*Solution:* $n = (0.8 \times 10^{-19}) / (1.6 \times 10^{-19}) = 0.5$. Since 0.5 is not an integer, this charge is IMPOSSIBLE.

> **Practice 2:** ?? **Medium** | Can a particle carry a charge of $4.8 \times 10^{-18} \text{ C}$? What about $6.0 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$?
<details>
<summary>View Solution</summary>
**Case 1:** $n = \frac{4.8 \times 10^{-18}}{1.6 \times 10^{-19}} = 30$. Since 30 is an integer, YES, it is possible.
**Case 2:** $n = \frac{6.0 \times 10^{-19}}{1.6 \times 10^{-19}} = 3.75$. Since 3.75 is a fraction, NO, it is impossible.
</details>

### Type 3: Alpha Particles & Nucleus Charges
**The Pattern:** You need to know the charge of specific subatomic particles.
*   Alpha ($\alpha$) particle: Helium nucleus, has 2 protons. Charge = $+2e$.
*   Nucleus of an atom (atomic number Z): Charge = $+Ze$.

> **Practice 3:** ?? **Easy** | What is the total charge of a Carbon nucleus (Atomic number Z = 6)?
<details>
<summary>View Solution</summary>
A nucleus only contains protons and neutrons. Protons have charge $+e$, neutrons are neutral.
$q = +Ze = +6e$
$q = +6 \times (1.6 \times 10^{-19}) = +9.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$.
</details>

### Type 4: Rate of charge transfer (Time problems)
**The Pattern:** They give you a rate (electrons per second) and ask for the time to reach a target charge.
**Formula:** Target Charge = (Rate of electrons/sec) $\times e \times$ Time

> **Practice 4:** ?? **Medium** | ? **Must-Do** | If $10^9$ electrons move out of a body every second, how much time is required to get a total charge of $1 \text{ C}$ on the body?
<details>
<summary>View Solution</summary>
1. Calculate charge moving per second: 
   $q/\text{sec} = 10^9 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} = 1.6 \times 10^{-10} \text{ C/s}$
2. Calculate time: 
   $t = \frac{\text{Total Charge}}{\text{Charge per sec}} = \frac{1}{1.6 \times 10^{-10}}$
   $t = 6.25 \times 10^9 \text{ seconds}$.
*(Note: To convert to years, divide by $365 \times 24 \times 3600$. It's roughly 198 years!)*
</details>

## ?? Stage 4: Type Mixer

> **Mixer 1:** ?? **Medium** | A piece of polythene is rubbed with wool, acquiring a negative charge of $3.2 \mu\text{C}$. Find the number of electrons transferred, and state whether the mass of the polythene increased or decreased.
<details>
<summary>View Solution</summary>
**Part 1 (Type 1):** 
$q = 3.2 \mu\text{C} = 3.2 \times 10^{-6} \text{ C}$
$n = \frac{q}{e} = \frac{3.2 \times 10^{-6}}{1.6 \times 10^{-19}} = 2 \times 10^{13}$ electrons.

**Part 2 (Type 3 Concept):**
Because the polythene acquired a *negative* charge, it gained electrons. Since electrons have mass, the mass of the polythene **increased**.
Increase in mass $\Delta m = n \times m_e = (2 \times 10^{13}) \times (9.1 \times 10^{-31}) = 1.82 \times 10^{-17} \text{ kg}$.
</details>

## ?? Stage 5: Board Arsenal (CBSE / State Board PYQs)

These questions test your exact phrasing. For board exams, **how you write the theory is as important as the final answer.**

> **Board Q1:** ?? **Easy** | ? **Must-Do** | Explain the meaning of the statement 'electric charge of a body is quantised'. (CBSE 2019, 2021)
<details>
<summary>View Solution</summary>
**Board Perfect Answer:** 
The statement means that the total electric charge on a body is always an integral multiple of a basic quantum of charge, which is the charge on an electron ($e$). 
Mathematically, $q = \pm ne$, where $n$ is an integer ($1, 2, 3\dots$) and $e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$. Charge cannot exist in fractional multiples of $e$.
</details>

> **Board Q2:** ?? **Medium** | Why can one ignore quantisation of electric charge when dealing with macroscopic i.e., large scale charges? (CBSE 2020)
<details>
<summary>View Solution</summary>
**Board Perfect Answer:**
At the macroscopic level, we deal with charges that are extremely large compared to the elementary charge $e$ ($1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$). Because $e$ is infinitesimally small, the discrete nature of charge is not noticeable, and the charge appears to be continuous (just like how a line drawn by a pencil appears continuous, even though it's made of billions of separate carbon atoms). Therefore, for practical purposes on a large scale, we can ignore quantisation.
</details>

> **Board Q3:** ?? **Medium** | A glass rod when rubbed with silk cloth acquires a charge of $+3.2 \times 10^{-7} \text{ C}$. (i) Calculate the number of electrons transferred. (ii) Is there a transfer of mass from glass to silk? (CBSE 2015)
<details>
<summary>View Solution</summary>
**(i)** Number of electrons transferred, $n = \frac{q}{e}$
$n = \frac{3.2 \times 10^{-7}}{1.6 \times 10^{-19}} = 2 \times 10^{12}$ electrons.

**(ii)** Yes. Since electrons are physical particles with mass ($9.1 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$), and the glass rod became positive (lost electrons), the electrons transferred to the silk. Therefore, mass transferred from glass to silk.
Amount of mass transferred = $n \times m_e = 2 \times 10^{12} \times 9.1 \times 10^{-31} = 1.82 \times 10^{-18} \text{ kg}$.
</details>

## ?? Stage 6: JEE Mains Arena

JEE Main questions combine concepts and test if you understand the limits of formulas.

> **JEE Q1:** ?? **Hard** | A copper penny (mass = $3.11 \text{ g}$) is electrically neutral. Suppose we somehow remove $10^{10}$ electrons from it. What fraction of the total number of electrons in the penny was removed? (Atomic mass of Cu = 63.5 g/mol, Atomic number Z = 29, $N_A = 6.023 \times 10^{23}$)
<details>
<summary>View Solution</summary>
**Step 1: Find total atoms in the penny.**
Number of moles = $\frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Molar Mass}} = \frac{3.11}{63.5} = 0.04898 \text{ moles}$.
Total atoms = Moles $\times N_A = 0.04898 \times 6.023 \times 10^{23} = 2.95 \times 10^{22} \text{ atoms}$.

**Step 2: Find total electrons originally present.**
Since Cu has Z=29, each atom has 29 electrons.
Total electrons = $29 \times (2.95 \times 10^{22}) = 8.55 \times 10^{23} \text{ electrons}$.

**Step 3: Find the fraction removed.**
Fraction = $\frac{\text{Electrons removed}}{\text{Total electrons}} = \frac{10^{10}}{8.55 \times 10^{23}} \approx 1.16 \times 10^{-14}$.

*Key Takeaway:* Even when removing 10 billion electrons, the fraction of total electrons removed is almost zero. This proves exactly why macroscopic bodies appear neutral and why charge feels continuous!
</details>

> **JEE Q2:** ?? **Hard** | Two identical metallic spheres A and B carry charges $+Q$ and $-2Q$ respectively. They are brought in contact and then separated. What is the new charge on each sphere, and how many electrons were transferred during the contact process in terms of Q and e?
<details>
<summary>View Solution</summary>
**Step 1: Find final charge (Conservation and Additivity)**
When identical spheres touch, the total charge is distributed equally.
Total initial charge = $(+Q) + (-2Q) = -Q$.
Final charge on each = $\frac{-Q}{2} = -0.5Q$.

**Step 2: Find the transfer of electrons**
Look at sphere A. It went from $+Q$ to $-0.5Q$.
Change in charge $\Delta q = \text{Final} - \text{Initial} = -0.5Q - (+Q) = -1.5Q$.
Since it became more negative, it *gained* electrons.
The magnitude of charge transferred is $1.5Q$.
Number of electrons transferred $n = \frac{\text{Charge transferred}}{e} = \frac{1.5Q}{e}$.

*Trap avoided:* Students often just subtract the magnitudes or divide $Q/e$ without finding the delta. Always track initial $\rightarrow$ final states!
</details>
