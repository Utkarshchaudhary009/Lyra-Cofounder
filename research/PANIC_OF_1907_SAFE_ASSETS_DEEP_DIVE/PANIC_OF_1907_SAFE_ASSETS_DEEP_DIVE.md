## EXECUTIVE OVERVIEW

### What Happened?
The Panic of 1907 was the **first major global financial crisis of the 20th century**. It lasted about 3-6 weeks (concentrated in October-November 1907) but its effects rippled for years. The stock market fell almost 50% from its peak the year before. Over 25 banks and 17 trust companies went bust.

### Why It Matters Today
- It **directly led to the creation of the Federal Reserve System** in 1913
- Shows how crises can spread from "shadow banks" (institutions outside the main safety net)
- Reveals the psychology of financial panic - fear, rumors, loss of confidence
- Demonstrates the critical role of **trusted leadership** in stabilizing markets
- Shows how a **real shock** (earthquake) can trigger a financial cascade years later

### The Big Picture Timeline
```
April 1906: San Francisco Earthquake hits
  ↓
Late 1906: British insurers send MASSIVE gold payments to US
  ↓
Summer 1907: Credit crunch, Bank of England raises rates, US enters recession
  ↓
October 16, 1907: Heinze & Morse fail at cornering copper stock
  ↓
October 21-22: Knickerbocker Trust fails after runs
  ↓
October 24+: J.P. Morgan mobilizes bankers to rescue system
  ↓
November 1907: Crisis stabilizes, but economic damage is severe
  ↓
1908-1913: Years of investigation and institutional reform
```

## WHAT IS A SAFE ASSET?

### Simple Definition
A **safe asset** is something you buy/hold when you're scared of losing money. When people panic, they move their money OUT of risky investments and INTO safe assets.

### Characteristics of Safe Assets
(What makes an investment feel safe to people?)

1. **Liquidity** - You can quickly turn it into cash
2. **Functionality** - It has real, lasting demand
3. **Limited Supply** - Supply doesn't grow faster than demand
4. **Certainty of Demand** - It won't become worthless
5. **Permanence** - It doesn't decay or rot

### Examples of Safe Assets (Modern)
- **Gold** - Physical commodity, can't be printed, history as store of value
- **US Treasury Bills** - Backed by US government, government won't default
- **Cash** - Most liquid, though loses value to inflation
- **Japanese Yen** - Japan is neutral in geopolitics, stable government
- **Swiss Franc** - Switzerland political neutral, strong banking system
- **Defensive Stocks** - Utilities, healthcare, food companies (always needed)

### The Critical Insight
**What counts as "safe" changes depending on what people are afraid of!**

For example:
- During a recession → Government bonds are safe (interest rates drop)
- During a war/geopolitical crisis → Bonds might NOT be safe (country might print money)
- During a currency crisis → Foreign currency might be safest
- During a stock crash → Cash is safest

### Safe Assets in 1907
People didn't have many options:
- **Cash** was safest but hard to get
- **Gold** was valuable and safe but scarce
- **Government bonds** existed but weren't as popular
- **Bank deposits** seemed safe... until banks failed
- **Stocks** became VERY risky

## THE PANIC OF 1907: THE SETUP

### The Good Times (1897-1906)

The decade before 1907 was **absolutely booming**. Understand this: people thought nothing bad could happen.

**Economic Conditions:**
- Strongest growth period in US history up to that point
- Unemployment was below 3%
- Corporations were growing massively
- Fortunes were being made in railroads, steel, manufacturing

**The Mood:**
- Optimism everywhere
- People believed in America's unlimited growth
- Investors were taking HUGE risks with borrowed money
- Companies were buying other companies (consolidation)

**The Problem:**
All this success was built on **borrowed money from Europe**. US corporations borrowed approximately $500 million (enormous at the time) from European banks to fuel expansion.

### Building Debt (The Risk Nobody Wanted to See)

**What was happening:**
1. American companies would borrow from European (especially British) banks
2. Use that borrowed money to buy stocks and do massive acquisitions
3. Stock prices went up because of all this buying
4. Everyone felt rich and smart

**The Hidden Danger:**
- This was **debt-financed growth** - artificial boost from borrowing
- If credit dried up, companies couldn't repay
- If stock prices fell, the collateral used for loans would be worthless

Think of it like: You borrow $100,000 on your credit card at high interest. You "invest" it in stocks. Stocks go up so you feel genius. But you're now deeply indebted...

### The Stress on the System

By summer 1906, warning signs appeared:
- In June 1906: A big bond offering from NYC failed to sell - people ran out of money
- July 1906: Copper market collapsed
- August 1906: Standard Oil fined $29 million for antitrust violations
- Stock prices fell 18% between January and July 1906

**Market Psychology Shift:**
> "The market keeps unstable...no sooner are these signs of new life in evidence than something like a suggestion of a new outflow of gold to Paris sends a tremble all through the list" 
> — *The Commercial & Financial Chronicle*, July 27, 1906

People were **nervous**. Something felt off. But nobody wanted to admit it.

## THE EARTHQUAKE CONNECTION

### April 18, 1906: San Francisco Earthquake

**What Happened:**
- 8.3 magnitude earthquake at 5:12 AM
- 296 miles of the San Andreas Fault ruptured at once
- The shaking lasted 55 seconds but felt eternal
- Massive fires broke out (4 days and nights of burning)
- Result: 490 city blocks destroyed, ~25,000 buildings gone, ~3,000 people dead

**Why It Mattered to Finance:**
San Francisco wasn't just a city - it was **the financial hub of the Pacific**. It had:
- Major financial connections to Britain
- Most west coast wheat shipped through there financed by London banks
- Major British insurance companies had offices there

### The Insurance Claim Bomb

**Here's the key detail most people miss:**

In 1906, **almost nobody in San Francisco had earthquake insurance** (because it didn't exist!). But almost EVERYONE had **fire insurance** - and the fires came AFTER the quake. So insurance companies had to pay out for fire damage.

**The Numbers:**
- Estimated damage: $350-500 million (1.3-1.8% of US GNP!)
- British insurance companies had written most policies: ~£23 million ($108 million)
- These were MASSIVE liability claims

**What British Insurance Companies Did:**
To pay claims in San Francisco, British insurers loaded **gold onto ships** and sent it to America. This created a gold flow:
- April 1906: $30 million in gold sent
- Summer 1906: More shipments
- September 1906 alone: $35 million in gold

**Total British gold sent: Equal to 14% of Britain's ENTIRE gold reserves!**

### The Monetary Shock: The Chain Reaction

This massive gold outflow created a crisis in Britain:

**Step 1: Gold Drain**
- British insurance companies needed to replace the gold they shipped out
- Gold stopped flowing INTO Britain naturally
- Britain was **losing gold reserves rapidly**

**Step 2: Bank of England's Panic Response**
To stop more gold from leaving Britain, the Bank of England **raised interest rates dramatically**:
- Raised rates from 3.5% to 6.0% between September-November 1906
- Increase of 250 basis points! (Huge move)

**Step 3: Credit Freeze in US**
The higher rates made it:
- More expensive for Americans to borrow from European banks
- Harder for corporations to roll over existing debt
- Banks stopped lending

**Step 4: US Recession**
By June 1907, the US fell into **one of the shortest but most severe recessions** in its history:
- Stock prices had been depressed all year
- Credit was tight
- Companies couldn't borrow
- Layoffs were starting

### Key Insight: A Real Shock Triggers Financial Crises

The San Francisco earthquake wasn't a financial event - it was a **real physical event**. But because the global economy was so interconnected through trade and finance:

1. Physical destruction in San Francisco
2. Insurance claims created gold transfers
3. Gold transfers affected British finances  
4. British rate increases affected US credit
5. Credit crunch put US into recession
6. Recession set the stage for panic

**This shows**: Financial crises often START from real shocks in the physical world, not just from speculation or greed.

## THE TRIGGER: UNITED COPPER SCHEME

### The Players

**F. Augustus Heinze**
- Age: Mid-40s
- Background: Made money in copper mining in Montana
- Personality: Aggressive, ambitious, reckless
- Goal: Get very, very rich quickly

**Charles W. Morse**
- Also a banker and speculator
- "Extreme character, even by American speculative standards"
- Had fingers in many financial pies

**Their Scheme:**
Attempt to "corner" United Copper Company stock - meaning:
- Buy up as much of the stock as possible
- Create artificial scarcity
- Drive the price up
- Sell at huge profit

(This is basically a massive pump-and-dump scheme)

### What Went Wrong

**October 15-16, 1907:**
- Heinze and Morse didn't have enough money/stock to actually corner the market
- The scheme **failed spectacularly**
- United Copper stock PLUMMETED instead of rising
- Heinze and Morse lost enormous sums

### The Contagion Begins

Because Heinze and Morse were bankers (not just speculators):
- They had borrowed money from banks using the stocks as collateral
- When stocks fell, the collateral became worthless
- Banks that lent to them were now in trouble

**The Mental Shift:**
Before: "We're in a boom, nothing can go wrong"  
After: "Wait... maybe these guy are fraudsters... maybe other banks are too..."

**Fear spread like wildfire:**
- Depositors started asking: "Is MY bank safe?"
- People remembered they weren't actually guaranteed their deposits
- Lines formed at banks as people rushed to withdraw their money
- This is called a "run on the bank"

## THE CRISIS CASCADE

### The Timeline of Terror

#### Week 1: October 16-20, 1907

**October 16:**
- Heinze/Morse copper scheme collapses
- Stock prices plummet

**October 18:**
- News breaks that Charles Barney (president of Knickerbocker Trust) is an associate of Morse
- Immediate run on Knickerbocker Trust begins
- People line up to withdraw their deposits

**October 20:**
- National Bank of Commerce (which was clearing Knickerbocker's checks) STOPS doing so
- This means checks written by Knickerbocker clients bounced
- Panic intensifies

**October 21:**
- New York Clearing House (the private lender of last resort) **refuses to help Knickerbocker**
- Reason: Knickerbocker isn't a member - they only help member banks
- Knickerbocker's president Charles Barney resigns in disgrace

#### Week 2: October 22-24, 1907

**October 22:**
- **Knickerbocker Trust Company suspends operations**
- This is the "Lehman moment" of 1907
- Like the failure of Lehman Brothers in 2008, this sparked panic throughout the system
- Over the day, about $8 million was withdrawn from Knickerbocker

**October 23:**
- **Run spreads to other trust companies**
- Trust Company of America (biggest trust) gets crushed with withdrawals
- Deposits drop from $21 million to $6 million in 24 hours
- Other banks in trouble too

**October 24:**
- **Stock market in freefall**
- Call money rates (overnight lending rates) spike insanely:
  - Started at 9.5%
  - Jumped to 70%
  - Reached 100% (people literally wouldn't lend at normal rates anymore)
  - Sometimes NO lending happened at all
- **JP MORGAN ACTS**

### Interest Rate Explosion

Here's what 100% interest rate means: If you borrow $100, you owe $200 tomorrow. Obviously, nobody would borrow at this rate. The system was breaking.

**Why rates went so high:**
- Stock brokers needed overnight credit to function
- When they couldn't get credit, they couldn't buy stocks for clients
- Stock prices fell further
- People panicked more
- Money just... disappeared from availability

## TRUST COMPANIES: THE SHADOW BANKS OF 1907

### What Were Trust Companies?

**Trust companies** were financial institutions that operated kind of like banks but weren't regulated like banks.

**What they did:**
1. Took customer deposits (like banks)
2. Made loans (like banks)
3. But were lightly regulated (unlike banks)

**Why they existed:**
- Wealthy people wanted someone to manage their estates, trusts, etc.
- These institutions grew and started taking deposits from regular people
- They were called "trusts" because people trusted them

**The Problem:**
Just like modern "shadow banks," trusts:
- Weren't members of the Clearing House (no safety net)
- Didn't have enough cash reserves (only kept 5% cash vs. 25% for regular banks)
- Loaned tons of money to the stock market
- Were heavily connected to risky speculation

### How Trusts Fueled Stock Market (and Created Danger)

**The Credit Chain:**

```
1. Broker wants to buy stocks for client
   ↓
2. Broker borrows from Trust Company (uncollateralized, daily loans)
   ↓
3. Uses stocks he buys as collateral for a "call loan" from a bank
   ↓
4. Uses money from bank to repay Trust Company
   ↓
5. At end of day: Stocks owned, credit repaid, broker gets commission
```

This system worked GREAT when:
- Stock prices were stable
- Brokers could easily get credit
- Trust companies had cash

But if ONE thing broke:
- Stock prices fall → Collateral worthless
- Brokers can't repay loans
- Trusts run out of cash
- Trust deposits get called in
- Trust goes bust

### The Contagion Mechanism

**Unlike regular banks:**
- Trust companies had NO emergency lending from the Clearing House
- When one trust got in trouble, depositors RAN to other trusts
- Because people thought: "If one trust is bad, maybe they all are!"

**The Psychological Element:**
Information was **opaque**. Nobody really knew which trusts were solvent. So people made a simple rule:
- "Get my money out FIRST, ask questions later"
- This is called a "liquidity panic"

## JP MORGAN: LEADERSHIP IN CRISIS

### Who Was JP Morgan in 1907?

**Background:**
- Age: 70 years old (semi-retired)
- Wealth: Enormous, but not unlimited
- Experience: Had rescued the US Treasury in the Panic of 1893 
- Reputation: Most trusted financier in America
- Personality: Autocratic, brilliant, powerful

**His Bank:**
- J.P. Morgan & Co. was the most powerful private bank in America
- Morgan had fingers in railroads, steel, finance, everything

### Morgan's Intervention

#### October 19: Return to NYC

**The Setup:**
- Morgan was attending a church convention in Richmond, Virginia when the panic started
- As news worsened, he realized what was happening
- He left the convention and **rushed back to New York on the night of October 19**

#### October 20-24: Mobilizing

**Morgan's Strategy:**
1. Called meetings of NY's leading bankers to his library (his house!)
2. Examined the books of struggling trust companies (super fast analysis)
3. Decided which trusts to save
4. Coordinated rescue packages

**The Library:**
Morgan had a library at his house at Madison Ave & 36th Street. The front pages of newspapers literally described crowds of bankers and trust company presidents coming through his doors, asking for help.

#### October 22: The Knickerbocker Decision

Morgan examined Knickerbocker Trust and decided: **NOT WORTH SAVING**

Why? Because he had Benjamin Strong (future Federal Reserve chief) examine the books and concluded the institution's solvency was uncertain. So Morgan refused to help.

**This was controversial:**
- Some people later said Morgan engineered the panic to destroy trusts (his competitors)
- But in the moment, it seemed like a prudent decision
- Knickerbocker went under

#### October 23-24: Saving the Stock Market

**The Real Crisis:**
Even though Knickerbocker failed, Morgan realized the whole system was unraveling. The stock market was about to crash.

**Morgan's Actions:**
1. Called together major bankers and financiers
2. Started **personally providing cash** to brokerage houses
3. Had cash delivered directly to the NYSE trading floor
4. Convinced John D. Rockefeller to contribute ~$10 million
5. Got Treasury Secretary Cortelyou to inject $35 million

**The Psychology:**
The act of Morgan personally showing up with bags of cash and **demonstrating confidence** was huge:
- If Morgan believed the system would survive, maybe it would
- If the richest man in America thought banks were safe... maybe they were
- Confidence is self-reinforcing

#### October 24: The Famous Quote

When Morgan came out and said about the Trust Company of America:

> "This is the place to stop this trouble"

This became mythical in financial history. Morgan put his reputation and money on the line. 

### The Moral Dimension of Morgan's Actions

**What Morgan Did Well:**
- Used his reputation to calm panic
- Coordinated collective action (very hard to do)
- Prevented total systemic collapse
- Ensured payment of deposits (mostly)

**What Was Controversial:**
- His bank benefited from the rescue (competitors were weakened)
- Trust companies (his rivals) were destroyed
- He used this to consolidate power
- Years later, the Pujo Committee investigated whether he engineered the panic

**The Deeper Issue:**
A single person shouldn't have to save the financial system with his personal wealth. This is why the Federal Reserve was created.

### Mental Model: JP Morgan

**How Morgan Thought:**
1. **Character is everything** - In his famous Pujo Committee testimony, he said: "The first thing is character... before money or anything else"
2. **Information matters** - He obsessively gathered information to understand which institutions were actually solvent
3. **Collective action is hard** - He personally coordinated the bankers' rescue, showing how difficult coordination is in a crisis
4. **Confidence is real** - He understood that showing confidence could actually CREATE confidence
5. **History taught lessons** - He'd seen the 1893 panic and learned what worked

## MENTAL MODELS

### What Are Mental Models?

A **mental model** is the way someone thinks about the world - their assumptions, beliefs, patterns they notice, lessons they've learned.

During crises, these mental models **break down or get overwhelmed** by new information and fear.

### The Mental Models of Different Groups

#### Depositors' Mental Model: "Banks Are Safe"

**The Belief:**
- My bank keeps my money safe
- I can always get my deposits when I want
- The bank is backed by smart, honest people

**What Broke:**
- October 1907: Knickerbocker, a major trust, **couldn't repay deposits**
- Depositors realized: Banks can FAIL
- The assumption was wrong

**The Panic:**
Once depositors realized banks weren't automatically safe:
- **Information asymmetry problem** - They didn't know which banks were actually solvent
- Simple heuristic: "Better get MY money out fast"
- Result: Bank runs

**The Psychology:**
This is pure **fear and uncertainty**:
- Don't know what you don't know
- Safer to assume worst-case
- Better to panic first and ask questions later

#### Stock Brokers' Mental Model: "Credit Is Always Available"

**The Belief:**
- We can always get overnight credit
- Call loans are always available
- Stock collateral is always acceptable

**What Broke:**
- October 24: Call loan rates spike to 100%
- People stop lending at any rate
- Credit... disappears

**The Response:**
Brokers couldn't function because:
- They couldn't buy stocks for clients
- They couldn't finance their own positions
- They're just... stuck

#### Businesspeople's Mental Model: "Growth Forever"

**The Belief** (from 1897-1906):
- The economy only grows
- Debt-financed expansion is normal
- We can always borrow more

**What Broke:**
- Credit dried up
- Companies couldn't borrow to meet payroll
- Suddenly, all that debt is impossible to repay

**Real Effects:**
- Factories closed
- 25+ banks failed
- 17 trust companies failed
- Unemployment went from 3% to 8%
- Immigration dropped (people left because jobs disappeared)

#### Newspaper Publishers' Mental Model: "Trust the Leaders"

Two major newspapers covered the 1907 panic very differently:

**The Wall Street Journal:**
- Reported facts without much criticism
- Trusted that bankers knew what they were doing
- Assumed free market would work it out
- Very favorable coverage of Morgan and bankers

**The New York Times:**
- Covered the human drama
- Emphasized emotional panic as a real force
- Was more critical of excessive consolidation
- Showed how ordinary people were suffering

**The Difference:**
- WSJ readers: Wealthy people, financial interests - they trusted the system would protect them
- NYT readers: Middle class, workers - they were scared the system was rigged

## GLOBAL REACTIONS

### Why It Was Global

The Panic of 1907 spread because:
1. Gold flows connected financial systems
2. British insurance companies invested in US stocks
3. International trade financing depended on credit
4. Banks in multiple countries had exposure

### International Bank Runs

The panic wasn't confined to America. **Financial crises happened in multiple countries:**

- **Egypt:** April-May 1907 (before the US crisis)
- **Japan:** May-June 1907
- **Germany:** Early October 1907
- **Chile:** Early October 1907
- **Canada:** Also affected

This shows **contagion** - problems in one country spread to others.

### Europe's Reaction

#### Bank of England

**Initial Response:**
- Raised discount rates to stop gold outflow (June 1906)
- This was **contractionary** - made credit tighter

**Later Response:**
- Eventually had to reverse course
- Allowed some gold to flow to US (to resolve the crisis)
- Gold imports helped stabilize US markets

#### Bank of France

Research shows the Bank of France actually played an important role:
- Sent gold to the US in September 1907
- This helped ease the credit crunch
- The Panic might have been worse without French help

**Key Insight:** Central banks eventually realized they needed to **loosen, not tighten** during crises.

### London Banking Centers

British banks and financiers:
- Had invested heavily in US securities
- Were exposed to the stock market crash
- Suffered real losses

But London's mature financial system had:
- More regulatory structure
- Better information
- More experienced operators
- So the crisis didn't hit as hard

### Japan's Response

Japan was interesting:
- Had bank runs in May-June 1907
- But recovered relatively quickly
- Japan wasn't as economically integrated with the US yet
- So contagion was less severe

**Later in 1908:**
- US business delegation went to Japan
- Goal: Convince Japan to help resolve the 1907 panic
- The "money trust" extended internationally

## KEY LESSONS & TAKEAWAYS

### What the Panic Reveals About Safe Assets

#### 1. Safe Assets Are Contextual

**Lesson:** What's "safe" depends on what people are afraid of

During the 1907 Panic:
- Bank deposits were NOT safe (banks were failing)
- Stocks were NOT safe (prices collapsed)
- Call loans were NOT safe (interest rates went to 100%)
- **Gold was safest** (physical, can't be printed, globally valued)
- **Cash was safest** (if you could get it)

Modern parallel:
- During a recession: Treasury bonds are safe (prices go UP as rates fall)
- During inflation: Gold is safe (holds value)
- During currency crisis: Foreign currency is safe
- During geopolitical crisis: Neutral countries' assets are safe

#### 2. The Information Problem

**Lesson:** When you don't know what's really happening, fear takes over

In 1907:
- People didn't actually know which banks were solvent
- Nobody had clear information
- So people assumed the worst
- This created self-fulfilling prophecy (rumors CAUSED failures)

Modern parallel:
- 2008 crisis: People didn't know which banks had toxic assets
- COVID crisis: People didn't know which companies could survive lockdowns
- Fear of the unknown > actual risk

**Solution:** Transparency matters enormously during crises

#### 3. Safe Assets Require Trust in Institutions

**Lesson:** A safe asset is only safe if people believe the institution behind it is solid

Examples:
- Gold is safe because it's physical (can't be faked)
- US Treasury bonds are safe because people believe US won't default
- Japanese Yen is safe because Japan is stable
- But bank deposits are only safe if people trust the bank

In 1907:
- Trust companies CLAIMED to be safe
- But when one failed, people panicked about ALL of them
- Trust (in the institution) broke

**Critical Point:** Safe assets are actually "confidence assets" - they work because people think they'll work.

#### 4. Liquidity Can Disappear Instantly

**Lesson:** Just because an asset is normally liquid (easy to sell) doesn't mean you can always sell it

In 1907:
- Stocks are normally liquid - you can sell them anytime
- October 24: Can't sell at any reasonable price
- Brokers couldn't sell because nobody was buying
- **The market stopped functioning**

Modern parallel:
- 2008: Credit markets froze, even between banks
- Normally liquid assets became totally illiquid
- This is the concept of "liquidity crisis"

**Important:** Just because something CAN be sold doesn't mean WILL be sold in a panic.

#### 5. Leverage Is Dangerous in Crises

**Lesson:** When you borrow money to invest, and prices fall, you get wiped out

In 1907:
- Brokers, trusts, and corporations used enormous leverage
- Stock prices fell 50%
- Leveraged positions went to zero (and below)
- Leverage amplified losses across the system

Trust Company Structure:
- Hold 5% cash, 95% invested/loaned
- When 5% leaves, the system breaks
- This is the leverage trap

Modern parallel:
- 2008: Leverage in mortgage market brought down entire economy
- Banks with 20:1 or 30:1 leverage went bankrupt
- Leverage is a hidden risk in good times, killer in bad times

#### 6. Interconnectedness Creates Contagion

**Lesson:** When financial institutions are tightly connected, failure spreads

The Chain:
```
Heinze/Morse fail
  ↓
Banks that lent to them fail
  ↓
Trust companies exposed to banks fail
  ↓
Trust companies can't lend to stock market
  ↓
Stock brokers fail
  ↓
Brokerage houses fail
  ↓
Corporations can't get short-term credit
  ↓
Real economy crashes
```

Modern parallel:
- 2008: Lehman failure triggered AIG failure triggered more bank failures
- Each bank was connected to many others
- Failures created cascades
- This is called "systemic risk"

### What the Panic Reveals About Crisis Psychology

#### 1. Fear Is Contagious

**What Happened:**
- October 20: Small run on Knickerbocker
- October 22: Knickerbocker fails
- October 23: Runs spread to other trusts
- October 24: Stock market in freefall

**The Psychology:**
News of one failure → Fear spreads → Run on other institutions → They fail for real (self-fulfilling)

#### 2. Rumors Matter More Than Facts

**Contemporary Accounts:**
The research by Fohlin, Gehrig, and Haas found:
- Rumors about institutions drove runs
- NOT just about actual solvency
- "I heard Bank X has problems" caused Bank X to fail
- Even if the rumor was false!

**The Lesson:**
In a panic, **perception becomes reality**.
- If people think a bank will fail, it will fail
- Confidence is self-reinforcing (both ways)

#### 3. Information Asymmetry Creates Panic

**The Problem:**
- Depositors don't know the truth about their banks
- Banks don't want to share information (it might scare people)
- So people assume the worst
- Worst-case assumption becomes true

**Solution (from 1913):**
- Federal Reserve created to provide information
- Central bank could examine institutions
- FDIC insurance (created 1933) removed the information problem
- If deposits are guaranteed, people don't need to know the truth

#### 4. Leadership Matters Enormously

**JP Morgan's Role:**
- Examined books personally
- Made clear decisions about who to save
- Used his reputation to calm fears
- Coordinated collective action

**The Lesson:**
In a crisis, people look for someone they trust.
- If that person appears calm and capable → Confidence spreads
- If that person appears panicked → Fear spreads

Modern parallel:
- 2008: Ben Bernanke's calm communication helped stabilize markets
- During COVID: Clear, calm leadership from public health officials helped people comply with rules

### What This Teaches About Building Financial Systems

#### Lesson 1: Central Banks Are Necessary

**Why:**
- Panics happen when credit freezes
- Someone needs to provide emergency liquidity
- That someone can't be a private person (Morgan was too powerful)
- Need a public institution (Federal Reserve)

This is why the Panic of 1907 **directly led to the Federal Reserve's creation in 1913**.

#### Lesson 2: Regulation of Shadow Banking Is Important

**The Trust Company Problem:**
- Trust companies were "shadow banks" - financial but not regulated like banks
- They had the risk of banks but not the safety of banks
- When they failed, the whole system failed

**Modern Application:**
This is why after 2008, regulators focused on shadow banks:
- Money market funds
- Hedge funds
- Investment banks

Modern Financial Crisis had the SAME structure: unregulated financial institutions (investment banks) caused a crisis.

#### Lesson 3: Deposit Insurance Works

**Modern Solutions (created after great depression):**
- FDIC insurance guarantees deposits up to $250,000
- If a bank fails, depositors get their money anyway
- This STOPS bank runs

Why? Because people don't need to panic:
- "My deposits are insured, so even if bank fails, I'm okay"
- No incentive to run
- Without runs, banks can survive bad times

**Counterfactual:** If FDIC insurance existed in 1907, would the panic have happened?
- Probably not at the same scale
- Depositors wouldn't run if deposits were guaranteed
- Some trusts might have failed, but not the panic cascade

#### Lesson 4: Clearing Houses and Lenders of Last Resort

**New York Clearing House (1907):**
- Was like a proto-central bank
- Could issue "clearing house loan certificates"
- Provided emergency credit

**But it was limited:**
- Only helped member banks
- Refused to help Knickerbocker (not a member)
- Was coordinated by private bankers with their own interests

**The Federal Reserve (created 1913):**
- Fixed this by being public institution
- Could help ALL banks
- Could expand money supply in emergencies
- Wasn't motivated by private profit

## PSYCHOLOGY OF FINANCIAL PANIC: Why Smart People Became Fools

### Why Did Intelligent People Not See the Problem Coming?

**The answer: They couldn't use System 2 thinking when they needed it most.**

Nobel laureate Daniel Kahneman describes two brain systems:

**System 1 (Fast Brain):**
- Automatic, intuitive, quick
- Uses mental shortcuts ("heuristics")
- Relies on past patterns
- TAKES OVER during stress/urgency
- Makes ~35,000 decisions per day

**System 2 (Slow Brain):**
- Analytical, rational, careful
- Weighs evidence properly
- Takes time and energy
- Gets OVERWHELMED during crisis
- We forget to use it

**In 1907:**
- Boom times → People used System 1 ("Growth forever!" "Can't go wrong!")
- Crisis hits → System 1 panic takes over
- The analytical System 2 SHUTS DOWN under stress
- Result: Smart people making dumb decisions

---

### The Cognitive Biases That Caused the Panic

#### 1. **Overconfidence Bias** (Before Crisis)

**What It Is:**
People overestimate their knowledge and ability to predict the future.

**In 1907:**
- Bankers thought: "I understand this market"
- Businesspeople thought: "Growth will never stop"
- Investors thought: "I'm smarter than average"
- Trust company leaders thought: "We're too big to fail"

**The Science:**
Research shows intelligent people are MORE vulnerable to overconfidence. Why? Because they can **build logical arguments to defend wrong beliefs**.

Smart banker 1897: "The economy grew 30% last decade. Economics improves over time. Therefore, growth will continue forever. I'm a genius banker, so I see trends others miss."

✓ Sounds logical!
✗ Completely wrong!

**Result:** They leveraged like crazy, took insane risks, and built a house of cards.

---

#### 2. **Confirmation Bias** (Building to Crisis)

**What It Is:**
You seek information that confirms your beliefs and ignore contradictory evidence.

**In 1907:**
- Saw stock prices rising → "Markets are healthy!"
- Ignored copper prices falling → "Just a small sector issue"
- Saw credit tightening → "Just temporary"
- Bank of England raising rates → "Not about us"

**The Psychology:**
When you WANT something to be true (profits, growth), your brain actively FILTERS OUT bad news.

A banker sees:
- (Good news) "Market grew 5% last month"
- (Bad news) "Copper crashed 40%"
- Brain processes: "Market grew! See, I was right!"
- Brain filters: "Copper? Oh, that's just one sector..."

**Result:** When crisis came, they were shocked. How did they not see it? They DID see it, but their brains filtered it out.

---

#### 3. **Anchoring Bias** (During Crisis)

**What It Is:**
You rely too heavily on one piece of information (the "anchor").

**In 1907:**
- Depositors saw: "My bank was worth $50 million last year"
- Anchor = $50 million = "My bank is safe"
- Real problem: "But the bank LOST $10 million this month"
- Depositor ignores new information, anchors to old belief

**The Result:**
By the time they realized the truth, it was too late. The anchor was too strong.

**For investors:**
- Investor bought stock at $100
- Anchor = "I bought at $100"
- Stock falls to $30
- Instead of selling, they think: "It'll get back to $100"
- They hold, losing more money
- Smart? No. But anchoring makes you irrational.

---

#### 4. **Information Cascades** (Contagion Mechanism)

**What It Is:**
You make decisions based on what others are doing, not your own analysis.

**Simple Example:**
- Person A sees possible bank problems
- Person B observes Person A withdrawing money
- Person B thinks: "If A is withdrawing, maybe there IS a problem"
- Person B withdraws money
- Person C sees A and B withdrawing: "It must be real!"
- Person C withdraws
- EVERYONE withdraws, bank actually fails

**The Key:** Nobody really knows what's true. But everyone acts like the others know something.

**In 1907:**
```
October 16: Heinze/Morse fail (real problem)
October 18: Run on some banks (cascading)
October 20: Rumors spread (nobody knows facts)
October 22: EVERYONE withdraws (even from safe banks)
October 23: Safe banks fail because they can't meet withdrawals
```

**The cascading effect:** The rumors CREATED the crisis, even for banks that were actually solvent!

---

### The Neuroscience: Why Fear Hijacks the Brain

#### The Amygdala and Financial Panic

**What the Amygdala Does:**
- Processes fear and threat
- Activates "fight or flight"
- Triggers cortisol and adrenaline
- SHUTS DOWN rational thinking

**During Financial Crisis:**

```
October 16: NEWS - Heinze/Morse fail
    ↓
AMYGDALA ACTIVATES
    ↓
"THREAT DETECTED: Money at risk!"
    ↓
Cortisol floods bloodstream
    ↓
Prefrontal cortex (rational brain) SHUTS DOWN
    ↓
Person runs to bank, withdraws money
    ↓
"I'll analyze this rationally later"
```

**The Problem:**
You CAN'T analyze rationally when the amygdala is in charge. It's evolution: When facing a saber-tooth tiger, you RUN first, think later. But in financial crises, running makes it worse!

#### Scientific Evidence:

**Study 1: Stress and Financial Decisions**
- Researchers induced stress (cold water task) while people made investments
- Results: Stress made people MORE biased and LESS rational
- Under stress, people took MORE risk on losses, LESS risk on gains
- The reflection effect got WORSE under stress

**Implication for 1907:**
As panic spread, people got MORE scared → Made WORSE decisions → Panic spread → People got MORE scared. Vicious cycle.

**Study 2: Fear and Information Processing**
- Fearful people made worse decisions when given bad news
- Brain was too busy processing fear to process information
- Some people even became "contrarians" - taking HUGE risks to counteract fear

**Study 3: Brain Structure Changes**
- People experiencing financial hardship had PHYSICALLY SMALLER amygdala and hippocampus
- Financial stress literally changes brain structure
- This makes it even harder to think rationally!

---

### Why Intelligent People Were MOST Vulnerable

#### The Curse of Intelligence

**Paradox:** The smartest people sometimes make the WORST financial decisions.

**Why?**

Smart people can BUILD LOGICAL ARGUMENTS for wrong beliefs.

**Example:**

Dumb Person: "Market goes up. I buy stocks."

Smart Person: "Let me construct a logical argument..."
- Historical growth rates: 7% per year
- Innovation is accelerating
- Railroads will transform economy
- Leverage amplifies gains
- Therefore: 30% returns are justified
- Anyone not leveraging is irrational
- I'm intelligent, so I see what others miss

Smart Person SOUNDS more rational. But they built an elaborate cage of logic around a false belief.

When the crisis hit, they couldn't escape their own logic!

#### The Three Deadly Biases for Smart People

**1. Overconfidence**
- Smart people think they know more than they do
- They mistake intelligence for foresight
- "I'm smart" ≠ "I can predict the future"

**2. Confirmation Bias is MORE DANGEROUS for Smart People**
- Smart people can construct logical justifications for ignoring evidence
- They're better at rationalization
- They have more mental tools to dismiss contradictions

**3. Authority Bias**
- Smart people think other smart people know things
- "If J.P. Morgan thinks it's okay, it must be"
- They don't realize Morgan is also working with imperfect information

---

### The Physics of Panic: Loss Aversion & Fear

#### Loss Aversion

**The Principle:**
People feel losses **2x more intensely** than equivalent gains.

Gain $100: "Good"
Lose $100: "TERRIBLE" (feeling is 2x stronger)

**In 1907:**
- Deposit of $100 in "safe" bank
- Bank starts failing
- Possibility of LOSING $100
- Loss aversion ACTIVATES: "GET THE MONEY OUT NOW"
- Rational thought: "Wait, let me check if it's really failing"
- Brain: "NO TIME! POTENTIAL LOSS! RUN!"

**Result:** Perfectly rational people become irrational because loss aversion hijacks their brains.

#### The Stress Response

When stressed, your brain literally **can't process complex information**.

Under stress:
- Short-term memory drops 30%
- Processing speed slows 40%
- Emotional regulation fails
- You make decisions based on patterns, not analysis

In 1907, people under financial stress couldn't:
- Do complex math on bank solvency
- Weigh pros and cons carefully
- Think about long-term consequences
- Resist herding behavior

---

### Specific Psychological Failures in 1907

#### The Mental Models That Failed

**Depositors' Model:**
"Bank deposits are safe. Banks are run by trustworthy people."
- SHATTERED when Knickerbocker failed
- One bank failing = "Maybe ALL banks are failing"
- No rational basis, but fear doesn't need rationality

**Brokers' Model:**
"Credit is always available. Call loans are reliable."
- SHATTERED when call loan rates hit 100%
- Suddenly credit DOESN'T exist
- Their entire business model collapsed overnight

**Businesspeople's Model:**
"Debt is fuel for growth. We can always refinance."
- SHATTERED when banks stopped lending
- Couldn't pay payroll
- Had to lay off workers
- Companies went bankrupt

**Each failure reinforced the panic through information cascades.**

#### The Role of Rumors

**Key Finding from Research:**
Rumors CAUSED runs, not just solvency problems.

Example: Trust Company X might have been fine. But if people HEARD it was in trouble, they withdrew money, which MADE it fail.

**Psychology:**
- Information is incomplete
- People assume others know something
- Rumors fill the information gap
- Everyone acts on rumors as if facts
- Rumors become self-fulfilling prophecies

By the time people could verify information, the damage was done.

---

### The Leadership Factor: JP Morgan as Psychological Anchor

#### Why Morgan Worked

JP Morgan didn't just inject money. He provided:

**1. A Psychological Anchor (Positive)**
- His confidence became the new anchor
- Instead of "My bank might fail," people thought "JP Morgan believes in my bank"
- This shifted the information cascade

**2. Authority (Powerful)**
- He examined banks personally
- Made PUBLIC statements about solvency
- People TRUSTED him (unlike rumors)
- This provided missing information

**3. Action (Visible)**
- His presence at the NYSE showing up with cash
- Photos of money being delivered
- People SAW him acting confident
- This created a visual anchor

**The Neuroscience:**
Seeing authority figure act confident → Reduces amygdala activation → People can think rationally again.

---

### Why This Proves Humans Are Predictable (But Not Controllable)

#### The Scientific Consensus

Every major financial crisis shows the same psychological patterns:

**1. Overconfidence Boom Phase**
- Everyone thinks crisis can't happen
- Smart people especially confident
- Leverage increases

**2. Shock/Trigger**
- Something real happens (earthquake, bankruptcy)
- Amygdala activates across population

**3. Information Cascade**
- Rumors spread faster than facts
- People withdraw from "uncertain" institutions
- Solvent institutions fail due to runs
- This confirms people's fears (self-fulfilling)

**4. Panic Cycle**
- Fear → Worse decisions → More losses → More fear
- Vicious downward spiral

**5. Recovery When:**
- Authority figure acts confident
- New information comes
- Institutions stop the runs (deposit insurance, central bank)

This sequence has repeated:
- 1893 Panic (US)
- 1907 Panic (US)
- 1929 Great Depression
- 1987 Crash
- 1998 LTCM
- 2008 Financial Crisis
- 2020 COVID Crash (brief, stopped fast)

**The Pattern is SO predictable that we can model it mathematically.**

---

### What This Shows About Human Nature

#### The Good News: We Understand It

Every bias is now scientifically documented and measurable.

#### The Bad News: We Can't Overcome It Just by Knowing

Knowing about loss aversion doesn't make you immune to it.
Knowing about herding doesn't make you herd less.
Understanding the amygdala doesn't disable it during crisis.

#### The Real Solution

Systems, not individuals:
1. **Deposit Insurance** (stops bank runs regardless of fear)
2. **Lender of Last Resort** (Federal Reserve provides liquidity)
3. **Circuit Breakers** (automatic trading halts)
4. **Transparent Information** (rules requiring disclosure)
5. **Regulation** (prevents overleveraging)

These systems work BECAUSE they don't rely on rational behavior.

They work FOR the irrational brain, not against it.
