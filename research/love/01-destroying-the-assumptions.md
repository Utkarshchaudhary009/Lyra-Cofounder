# Chapter 1: Destroying the Assumptions

> *Before we talk about HOW to choose, we question WHAT we think we know.*

---

## 1.1 What Does "Choosing a Person" Actually Mean?

Most people hear this and think: *"I will find the right person and pick them."*

**But is that what actually happens?**

No. Three fundamental problems:

### Problem 1: It's Not One-Sided Selection

You are not a customer in a store. You don't browse people and select one. The other person is ALSO choosing. So "choosing a person" is really **mutual selection** — two people independently deciding the other is acceptable *enough* to continue with.

Your "choice" is constrained by who chooses you back. This means the quality of your options is directly tied to the quality of what you bring.

### Problem 2: You're Choosing Tradeoffs, Not a Person

Every human is a package deal. Their strengths are bundled with their weaknesses — often the same trait produces both:

- The ambitious person → also neglects you
- The easygoing person → also lacks drive
- The deeply emotional person → also overwhelmed easily
- The independent person → also hard to reach
- The spontaneous person → also unreliable with plans

You don't pick perfection. **You pick which set of imperfections you can live with for decades.**

### Problem 3: The Choice Is Not One-Time

You don't choose on a wedding day and it's done. You re-choose every morning. Every argument. Every boring Tuesday. Every crisis.

People who think choice is a single event are the ones blindsided when things fall apart. **Choice is a continuous act.** The question isn't "did I choose right?" — it's "am I still choosing this person today, and are they still choosing me?"

## 1.2 The Prerequisite Nobody Mentions

> **Before asking "who should I choose," ask: "Am I someone worth choosing?"**

If you skip this, the entire framework collapses. You attract what you are, not what you want.

**What "worth choosing" means practically:**
- Emotional stability (not perfection — stability)
- Financial trajectory (not wealth — direction)
- Physical health management
- Social competence
- Character (honesty, accountability, generosity)
- Purpose or direction in life

If you lack most of these, your pool of good options shrinks — not because good people are scarce, but because good people are also evaluating YOU.

---

## 1.3 Is There a "Right" Person?

**No.** This is the single most damaging myth in relationship thinking.

There is no single correct human for you. There are **degrees of compatibility** across multiple dimensions:

- Some people are compatible at 30% across the board
- Some are 90% compatible on values but 20% on lifestyle
- Some are 95% compatible but met at the wrong time

### What "right" actually means in practice:

Someone whose tradeoffs you can accept, whose growth trajectory is roughly parallel to yours, and who has the character to stay engaged when it stops being easy.

### The two catastrophic errors caused by the "one" myth:

1. **Premature rejection** — Discarding genuinely good partners because they don't match an imaginary ideal. No real human will match the composite image you built in your head from movies, books, and other people's highlight reels.

2. **Blind commitment** — Staying in terrible partnerships because you've decided this IS "the one" and leaving would mean failure. The sunk cost fallacy wearing a ring.

## 1.4 Can You Trust Your Own Judgment?

This is the question nobody asks. And the honest answer is: **not entirely.**

### Your judgment is compromised by:

**Biology:**
Hormones (oxytocin, dopamine, testosterone) literally cloud rational evaluation during early attraction. You are chemically impaired for roughly 6–18 months. You would not make a financial decision in this state, but you'll make a life decision.

**Attachment Patterns:**
Your childhood wiring makes certain dynamics feel like "home." If neglect was your childhood normal, emotionally unavailable people feel familiar. If chaos was normal, stable people feel "boring." **Familiar ≠ good.** Your nervous system does not distinguish between "this feels like home" and "this is healthy."

**Cognitive Biases:**
- *Confirmation bias:* Seeing only evidence that supports your existing choice
- *Sunk cost fallacy:* Staying because you've "invested" years and can't "waste" them
- *Halo effect:* They're attractive, so they must also be kind, smart, and honest
- *Projection:* Attributing YOUR qualities to them because you want them to match

**Social Pressure:**
- Age timelines ("You should be married by 28")
- Family expectations ("When are you settling down?")
- Peer comparison ("Everyone else has someone")
- Cultural scripts about what a relationship "should" look like

### The practical implication:

Your strongest "gut feeling" about a person in the first 6 months is the **least reliable data you have.** It's hormonal noise masquerading as intuition.

Real intuition about character comes after you've seen someone:
- Under stress
- In conflict with you
- In extended boredom
- Handling failure
- Holding power over someone weaker

## 1.5 The Knowing-Doing Gap

Most people encounter these ideas and... walk right back to what they were doing. Why?

**Is it that the answers don't exist? Or that the answers are uncomfortable and the brain rejects them?**

It's almost always the second.

The brain doesn't optimize for truth. It optimizes for **energy conservation and pain avoidance.** When you ask yourself "Is this person right for me?" and the honest answer is "No," that answer triggers:

1. **Anticipated loss** — Your brain models the pain of leaving (loneliness, withdrawal, identity disruption)
2. **Cognitive load** — Acting on the answer requires effort, planning, emotional labor
3. **Identity threat** — Admitting you chose wrong threatens your self-concept

So the brain does something elegant and terrible: **it reclassifies the question as "irrelevant" or "unanswerable."** Not because it IS — but because treating it as irrelevant costs zero energy and avoids pain.

### The counter-move:

Don't ask yourself "Is this person right for me?" in the abstract.

Instead ask: **"If my closest friend described this exact relationship to me, word for word, what would I tell them?"**

You already know the answer for others. The brain only blocks it when it's YOUR dopamine source at risk.

### The test for honest self-assessment:

If the answer to a hard question makes you feel **relief** — it's probably true.

If it makes you feel **nothing** and you "move on" immediately — your brain just deleted it for self-protection.

The answers that make you uncomfortable are usually the ones you need most.

## Chapter Summary

| Assumption | Reality |
|-----------|---------|
| "I choose a person" | Mutual selection — constrained by who chooses you back |
| "I pick the right one" | You pick a set of tradeoffs you can live with |
