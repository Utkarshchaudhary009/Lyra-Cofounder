# Deep Research Prompt: Steve Jobs' Philosophy on Brand Identity & Value Communication

## Primary Source
Steve Jobs' 1997 internal Apple marketing presentation (Think Different campaign launch)

---

## Research Directive
You are a Scientific researcher with zero biases.
Conduct rigorous, dispassionate analysis of Jobs' philosophy on brand identity, meaning-making, and corporate communication. Reject hagiography. Reject cynicism. Pursue structural understanding.

---

## Core Thesis to Interrogate

**Jobs' Claim:** In a noisy world, companies must communicate *who they are* (values/identity) rather than *what they make* (products/features). Nike exemplifies this—selling aspiration, not shoes. Apple's core value: "People with passion can change the world."

---

## Required Analysis Vectors

### 1. EPISTEMOLOGICAL EXAMINATION
- What does Jobs *actually* mean by "core values"? Is this tautological?
- Distinction between "speaking in words vs speech"—what is the operative difference?
- How does Jobs define "remembering" a company? Memory of what exactly—feeling, identity, utility?

### 2. CONTRADICTION MAPPING
- Jobs criticizes feature-based marketing, yet Apple later heavily marketed specs (processor speed, camera megapixels, chip names)
- "People with passion change the world"—but Apple's supply chain relied on workers with no agency to change anything
- Nike comparison: Nike faced sweatshop scandals. Does value-marketing obscure material realities?
- Jobs says values "shouldn't change"—but Apple's values shifted dramatically (closed ecosystem vs. early openness, privacy vs. China compliance)

### 3. BLIND SPOT IDENTIFICATION
- **Survivorship bias:** Jobs cites Nike/Got Milk as proof. What value-driven campaigns failed? Why ignore them?
- **Causation vs. correlation:** Did Nike succeed *because* of value-marketing or *despite* it? Or unrelated factors (athlete endorsements, product quality)?
- **The "Got Milk" paradox:** Jobs says milk campaign failed, then succeeded with "Got Milk"—but milk consumption continued declining post-campaign. Was this actually successful?
- **Self-referential logic:** "Crazy ones change the world" is unfalsifiable—those who failed weren't "crazy enough"

### 4. STRUCTURAL CRITIQUE
- Is "brand as identity" inherently manipulative? Creating emotional attachment to corporations?
- Does value-marketing obscure the transactional nature of commerce?
- Class dimension: Who gets to "change the world"? Jobs' philosophy assumes agency available to few
- The paradox of authenticity: If values are strategically deployed for profit, are they values or tactics?

### 5. HISTORICAL CONTEXT GAPS
- 1997: Apple near bankruptcy. Was this philosophy born of necessity (can't compete on features) rather than conviction?
- Jobs returned after being ousted. Personal redemption narrative possibly bleeding into corporate philosophy?
- Compare to Jobs' 1985-1996 period at NeXT—did he practice this philosophy there? Results?

### 6. ALTERNATIVE INTERPRETATIONS
- **Cynical read:** This is sophisticated manipulation—attach identity to products to create irrational loyalty
- **Pragmatic read:** Accurate observation of attention economics—differentiation through meaning
- **Sociological read:** Reflects shift from product-economy to experience-economy
- **Critical theory read:** Corporate capture of human meaning-making; commodification of aspiration

### 7. WHAT JOBS DOESN'T ADDRESS
- What happens when "core values" conflict with profit?
- Who decides what the "core values" are? Democratic or autocratic process?
- Can values exist without verification? Apple says X but does Y—which is the "real" value?
- The consumer's role: Are they participants or targets?

---

## Methodology Requirements

1. **Source triangulation:** Compare this speech to Jobs' other statements, biographies (Isaacson, Schlender), internal Apple documents, contemporary criticism
2. **Counter-evidence priority:** Actively seek examples that contradict Jobs' claims
3. **Steelman opposition:** Present the strongest possible critique of this philosophy
4. **Temporal analysis:** Track whether Apple followed this philosophy and outcomes
5. **Comparative cases:** Companies that tried value-marketing and failed; companies that succeeded with feature-marketing

---

## Output Constraints

- No hero narratives
- No villain narratives  
- No "genius" framing
- No "visionary" language
- State uncertainty explicitly
- Present competing interpretations without resolving
- Acknowledge what cannot be known

---

## Key Questions Without Clear Answers

1. Is value-marketing more honest (revealing what company truly believes) or less honest (hiding transactional nature)?
2. Did Apple succeed because of this philosophy, despite it, or independently of it?
3. Can a corporation have "values" in any meaningful sense, or only interests?
4. Is Jobs describing reality or constructing it through rhetoric?
5. What is the ethical status of creating emotional brand attachment?

---

## Warning Signs to Avoid

- Accepting Jobs' framing uncritically
- Assuming business success validates philosophy
- Confusing compelling rhetoric with valid argument
- Treating Apple's narrative about itself as historical fact
- Ignoring material conditions (labor, resources, power) in favor of ideas
