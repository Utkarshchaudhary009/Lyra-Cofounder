# "The Role of Youth in Local Governance and Policy-Making"

### *Addressed to students and teachers — 3 Minute Speech*
### *Utkarsh Chaudhary | Kanchanjanga House*

---

## The Speech (~480 words | ~3 minutes)

---

Good afternoon, respected teachers and friends. I am Utkarsh Chaudhary, proudly representing Kanchanjanga House.

*(pause — look at the audience — then begin slowly)*

---

Sir, ma'am, friends — last month, a gram sabha was held in a village near my town.

Decisions were made about a new school boundary wall, about where the public toilet would be built, about which road would get repaired first. Decisions that would affect every young person in that village — every student, every teenager, every child who walks that road to school.

I looked at the people making those decisions. The youngest person in that room was **forty-five.**

Not one student. Not one young farmer. Not one college-going boy or girl had a chair in that meeting. And nobody — **nobody** — noticed they were missing.

---

Now — I hear the argument. And I respect it.

*"Experience matters. Governance is not a game. You need years of understanding to make decisions that affect people's lives."*

And that is **true.** I am sixteen. I have not run a panchayat. I have not managed a budget. I have not dealt with contractors who promise a road and deliver a drain.

**I concede this.**

But let me ask you — respectfully — **whose experience are we talking about?**

The experience of people who make **Digital India** policy but have never sent an email without help? The experience of people who decide **education budgets** but have not sat in a classroom in thirty years? The experience of people who plan **youth employment schemes** and then set the application deadline during board exams?

**Experience without relevance is just age. And age, sir, is not a qualification. It is a calendar.**

---

India has **600 million people** under the age of twenty-five. That is not a minority. That is nearly **half the country.**

And yet — in panchayats, in ward meetings, in municipal bodies, in policy rooms — we are not at the table. We are not near the table. Most of the time, **we do not even know the table exists.**

We are old enough to vote at eighteen. We are old enough to join the army at seventeen. We are old enough to be tried as adults in court.

But we are not old enough to **have an opinion** about the road outside our own house?

---

> They call us the **future** of this country. But they will not let us into the **present.**
> They say **youth can change everything.** But they will not let youth change **anything.**

Sir — youth in governance is not rebellion. It is not impatience. It is not disrespect.

**It is responsibility.**

Because the people making decisions about our future should include at least **one person** who will actually have to live in it.

---

We are not asking for power. We are asking for a **chair.**

One chair. In one room. Where one young voice can say — *"Sir, this affects us. May we be heard?"*

**That is not too much to ask. That is the bare minimum a democracy owes its largest generation.**

---
